## When to Use

Use this when someone expresses the belief that wealth is extracted rather than created, or when they argue that rich people got rich by taking from the poor.
Also use it when someone asks why certain economies collapse despite abundant natural resources.
Naval's maker-taker framework provides a biological metaphor that explains why societies require a critical mass of creators to function, and what happens when parasitic elements grow too large.

## The Framework

Naval starts from a premise most people get wrong: wealth is not a fixed pie.

> "Obviously, there isn't a finite number of jobs, or finite amount of wealth. Otherwise we would still be sitting around in caves, figuring out how to divide up pieces of fire wood, and the occasional dead deer." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-03-ethical-wealth.md

All wealth in civilization has been created.
Technology, products, services -- all invented by makers.
The history of the world is a predator/prey relationship between those who create and those who take.

> "The history of the world, in some sense, is this predator/prey relationship between makers and takers. There are people who go out and create things, and build things, and work hard on things. Then there are people who come along with a sword, or a gun, or taxes, or crony capitalism, or Communism, or what have you." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-03-ethical-wealth.md

### The Parasite-Host Metaphor

Naval uses biology to explain the dynamic:

> "Even in nature, there are more parasites than there are non-parasitical organisms. You have a ton of parasites in you, who are living off of you. The better ones are symbiotic, they're giving something back. But there are a lot that are just taking. That's the nature of how any complex system is built." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-03-ethical-wealth.md

The mitochondria in your cells are symbiotes -- they help you survive.
Some regulation and governance is like mitochondria: necessary for the system to function.
But the critical threshold matters:

> "Any organism can only withstand a small number of parasites. When the parasitic element gets too far out of control, you die." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-05-free-markets.md

Applied to societies: when takers (through taxation, regulation, crony capitalism, corruption, or outright theft) outnumber makers, the society collapses.

> "Too many takers and not enough makers will plunge a society into ruin." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-05-free-markets.md

Naval cites Venezuela as the modern example: a country so focused on redistributing wealth that it destroyed the capacity to create it.

### The Ethical Distinction

The framework is not anti-government or anarchist.
Naval is careful to distinguish ethical wealth creation from crony capitalism, monopolies, and mispriced externalities.

> "I'm talking about free minds, and free markets. Small-scale exchange between humans that's voluntary, and doesn't have an outsized impact on others." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-05-free-markets.md

### The Abundance Argument

Everyone can be wealthy. The proof is historical.

> "Today, I would rather be a poor person in a First World country, than be a rich person in Louis the XIV's France. I'd rather be a poor person today than aristocrat back then. That's because of wealth creation." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-03-ethical-wealth.md

No antibiotics, no cars, no electricity, no smartphones.
All of that wealth was created by makers, not redistributed from a fixed pool.

## Example

A city imposes a new tax on tech companies to fund social programs.
The intent is good.
But the tax rate is so high that three of the five largest employers relocate.
Tax revenue drops below pre-tax levels.
Social programs are cut.
The remaining businesses face higher costs and reduced talent pools.
The takers drove out the makers, and everyone -- including the intended beneficiaries -- is worse off.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Explain why wealth creation is positive-sum and not zero-sum extraction
- Identify maker and taker dynamics in their own industry or community
- Understand the biological metaphor: parasites are inevitable, but the ratio matters
- Argue for ethical wealth creation without defending crony capitalism or monopolies
