## When to Use

Use this when someone is working extremely hard on a startup but not gaining traction, or when someone is evaluating multiple startup ideas and cannot decide.
Andreessen's product-market fit is a two-variable problem.
Naval expands it to three variables by adding the founder.
The founder's fit with the product and market is as important as the product's fit with the market.

## The Framework

Marc Andreessen popularized the concept of product-market fit: build something the market wants.
Naval adds a third variable that most founders ignore.

> "The most important thing for any company is to find product-market fit. But actually it's to find product-market-founder fit. Where you are naturally inclined to build the right product, which is also naturally inclined for the right market." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

### Three Variables, Not Two

**Founder fit:** Does this feel like play to you?
Do you have specific knowledge in this domain?
Would you work on this even if you were not getting paid?
If the founder is forcing themselves to care about the problem, they will eventually burn out or get outcompeted by someone who genuinely cares.

**Product fit:** Can you actually build this thing?
Do your skills, team, and resources match the product requirements?

**Market fit:** Does the market want this? Is the timing right? Are people willing to pay?

### All Three Must Align

Two out of three is not enough:
- Great founder + great market + wrong product = pivot needed
- Great founder + great product + wrong market = beautiful product nobody buys
- Great market + great product + wrong founder = someone else will win

### Hard Work Is Necessary but Not Sufficient

> "If getting wealthy is your goal, you're going to have to work as hard as you can. But hard work is no substitute for who you work with and what you work on." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

This is the strongest version of the argument.
The what (product), the who (market and team), and the fit (founder alignment) are the multipliers.
Hard work multiplied by zero is still zero.

### The Iterative Search

You do not find product-market-founder fit by analysis alone.
You find it by building, testing, and redefining.

> "Become the best in the world at what you do. Keep redefining what you do until this is true." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-13-redefining.md

### The Builder-Seller Dimension

Naval adds that every business needs someone who can build and someone who can sell:

> "Learn to sell, learn to build. If you can do both, you will be unstoppable." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-31-build-sell.md

The founder who understands both building and selling has a structural advantage in finding fit because they can iterate on both sides simultaneously.

### The Consensus Trap

The best startup ideas look weird to most people.
If everyone agrees your idea is great, the market is probably already crowded.

> "In entrepreneurship, the masses are never right." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-13-redefining.md

The weird idea that fits the founder perfectly is far more likely to succeed than the consensus idea that fits nobody in particular.

## Example

A former investment banker starts a fitness app because the market is large.
He has no specific knowledge in fitness, does not use fitness apps, and finds development tedious.
He works 80-hour weeks but the app is mediocre because he does not intuitively understand users.
A personal trainer with 5 years of client data and a genuine obsession with workout optimization builds a competing app.
She works 40-hour weeks but her product is better because the founder-product-market fit is tight.
She wins.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Evaluate their current venture across all three dimensions
- Identify which dimension is weakest and whether it can be fixed
- Stop using hard work as a substitute for fit
- Begin the iterative search of redefining what they do until all three variables align
