## When to Use

You have identified your specific knowledge — the thing that feels like play to you and looks like work to others. But you do not know where to apply it. Multiple markets and opportunities are available, and you are paralyzed by optionality. Or you are entering a competitive space and need to understand why previous attempts failed and what is different about your angle.

This framework connects two of the most powerful startup thinking tools from two different thinkers. Naval tells you WHAT you have (specific knowledge). Balaji tells you WHERE to deploy it (the idea maze). Together, they answer the full question: "What should I build, and why will it work this time?"

## The Two Frameworks

### Naval's Specific Knowledge

Specific knowledge is the foundation of wealth creation in Naval's system. It has five properties:

1. **Cannot be trained** — if a classroom can teach it, it is general knowledge
2. **Found by pursuing curiosity** — not career advice or market trends
3. **Feels like play** — the person would do it without payment
4. **Lives at the edge of knowledge** — where textbooks have not arrived yet
5. **Is highly creative or technical** — transmitted through apprenticeship, not instruction

> "Specific knowledge is found much more by pursuing your innate talents, your genuine curiosity, and your passion. It's not by going to school for whatever is the hottest job."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Arm Yourself With Specific Knowledge"

The problem with specific knowledge alone: it tells you what you are uniquely good at, but not where that uniqueness has economic value. Many people have genuine specific knowledge in domains where no market exists, or where the timing is wrong, or where the obvious application has already been captured by someone else.

### Balaji's Idea Maze

The idea maze (originally Chris Dixon's concept, extended by Balaji) is a framework for evaluating startup opportunities by mapping the full landscape of previous attempts.

The key questions:
- What companies have tried this before?
- Why specifically did each one fail?
- What has changed — technologically, socially, economically — since those failures?
- Which doors that were previously closed are now open?

Balaji's critical addition to the framework:

> "Just because a business proposition didn't work in the past doesn't necessarily mean it won't work today. The technological and social prerequisites may have dramatically changed, and doors previously closed may now have opened. Unlike the laws of physics, society is not time invariant."
> — Balaji Srinivasan, The Network State

The idea maze alone has a limitation: it tells you what paths are available but not which path YOU should take. Two equally valid paths through the maze might exist, but only one matches your specific combination of skills and obsessions.

## The Convergence

When you combine these two frameworks, you get a complete decision system:

**Specific knowledge** answers: What am I uniquely good at?
**The idea maze** answers: Where in the competitive landscape does my unique ability create an advantage?

### Step 1: Map Your Specific Knowledge

Use Naval's five-property test. Identify 2-3 candidates for your specific knowledge. Score each on the criteria. Focus on the one that scores highest on "feels like play" and "found by pursuing curiosity."

### Step 2: Enter the Maze with Your Knowledge as Your Compass

For each market or opportunity you are considering, map the idea maze:
- Name the companies that tried this before
- Identify why each failed (not vague — specific failure modes)
- Identify what has changed since they failed

Now the key move: for each open path in the maze, ask: **Does this path favor my specific knowledge?**

A path through the maze that requires deep understanding of consumer psychology AND technical product-building AND persuasive communication might be open — but it is only valuable to someone whose specific knowledge sits at that intersection. For everyone else, it is just another path they lack the skills to navigate.

### Step 3: Find the Destiny Luck Path

Naval's four types of luck connect directly to the idea maze:

- **Type 1 (blind luck)**: Stumbling into the right path randomly. In 1,000 parallel universes, you find it in maybe 10.
- **Type 2 (hustle luck)**: Running through the maze quickly, trying many paths. You find a good one through energy and persistence.
- **Type 3 (preparation luck)**: Your specific knowledge lets you see a path others miss. You notice the open door because you have been studying the lock for years.
- **Type 4 (destiny luck)**: Your specific knowledge IS the path. The opportunity exists specifically because of your unique combination, and it comes to you.

> "You build a unique character, a unique brand, a unique mindset, where then luck finds you."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Making Money Isn't About Luck"

The idea maze helps you move from Type 2 (running around trying things) to Type 3 (spotting the path others miss) by giving you the map. Your specific knowledge is what lets you read the map in a way others cannot.

### Step 4: The Authenticity Filter

Naval provides the final test:

> "Escape competition through authenticity."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Escape Competition Through Authenticity"

In the idea maze, the most crowded paths are the ones that look obvious with general knowledge. The paths that match your specific knowledge will be less crowded by definition, because they require YOUR unique combination to navigate. This is how authenticity eliminates competition: not through positioning tricks, but through genuine alignment between who you are and what you build.

## Example

A former documentary filmmaker has spent 10 years developing specific knowledge in: visual storytelling, interviewing reluctant subjects, and making complex topics emotionally compelling. She also has a side obsession with personal finance — she has read every book, built her own investment models, and taught friends how to invest.

The idea maze for personal finance content is crowded: Ramit Sethi, Dave Ramsey, dozens of YouTube channels. Most failed approaches tried to compete on information (more tips, more strategies). The winners compete on emotional connection and entertainment value.

She maps the open doors: short-form documentary-style finance content that makes viewers FEEL something rather than just learn something. Previous attempts by text-based finance creators to add video failed because they lacked production skills. Previous attempts by production companies to create finance content failed because they lacked genuine finance obsession.

Her specific knowledge sits exactly at the intersection the maze reveals: documentary craft + genuine finance obsession. She is not the best filmmaker. She is not the best finance mind. She is the only person with BOTH, applied to THIS path through the maze.

This is the convergence: the idea maze told her where the open door was. Her specific knowledge told her she was the one who could walk through it.

## Output

After reading this framework, you should be able to:
1. Map your specific knowledge and the idea maze for a given opportunity simultaneously
2. Filter open paths in the maze by whether they favor your specific combination
3. Move from Type 2 luck (hustle) to Type 3 luck (preparation) by studying the maze
4. Identify paths that are open to you specifically because of your unique skill combination
5. Apply the authenticity filter: if anyone else could take this path as easily as you, it is not the right path
