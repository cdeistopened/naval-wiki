## When to Use

Use this when you have a business idea that involves leverage (code, media, capital, or labor) and you need to evaluate whether the idea has been tried before, why previous attempts failed, and whether your specific knowledge gives you a unique path through the maze. Also use this when you feel stuck choosing between leverage types and need a framework for mapping the competitive landscape before committing.

## The Two Frameworks

### Naval's Leverage Stack

Naval identifies four types of leverage, ranked from worst to best:

1. **Labor** (oldest, worst) -- "Managing other humans is incredibly messy. It requires tremendous leadership skills."
2. **Capital** (permissioned) -- "To use it, you need someone's permission. You need someone to give you money."
3. **Code** (permissionless, egalitarian) -- "An army of robots is freely available -- it's just packed in data centers."
4. **Media** (permissionless, egalitarian) -- "You can create software and media that works for you while you sleep."

Naval's key insight: specific knowledge determines which leverage to pursue. "Specific knowledge is found by pursuing your innate talents, your genuine curiosity, and your passion. It's not by going to school for whatever is the hottest thing."

### Balaji's Idea Maze

Balaji's idea maze says that every worthwhile business idea exists inside a maze of previous attempts, dead companies, technology shifts, and competitive paths:

"Just because a business proposition didn't work in the past doesn't necessarily mean it won't work today. The technological and social prerequisites may have dramatically changed, and doors previously closed may now have opened. Unlike the laws of physics, society is not time invariant."

The founder quality test: "A good founder is capable of anticipating which turns lead to treasure and which lead to certain death."

## The Synthesis: Maze-Walking as Leverage Evaluation

The idea maze is the missing diagnostic for Naval's leverage framework. Naval tells you to pursue leverage aligned with your specific knowledge. But he doesn't give you a method for scanning the landscape to see who else has tried, what killed them, and what's changed. Balaji does.

Here's how they combine:

### Step 1: Name Your Specific Knowledge

Start with Naval's framing. Your specific knowledge is the intersection of your innate talents, accumulated experience, and genuine curiosity. It's what feels like play to you but looks like work to others.

Write it down as a sentence: "I know how to [X] because of [Y experience] and I find [Z] genuinely fascinating."

This is your compass in the maze.

### Step 2: Map the Dead Companies

For each leverage type you're considering (code, media, capital, labor), map the companies that already tried this:

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Who tried to build this with code? | Which technical approaches failed and why? |
| Who tried to build this with media? | Which audiences were too small, which monetization models broke? |
| Who tried this with capital? | Did they fail because the idea was wrong or because capital alone couldn't solve the distribution problem? |
| Who tried this with labor? | Was the labor model unsustainable? Would code or media replace the labor component today? |

Balaji insists on specificity here. Not "some companies tried this." Name them. Name why each one died. "A bad founder just has a vague idea of 'treasure somewhere in the maze' without understanding the full landscape."

### Step 3: Identify the Technology Shifts

Balaji's critical addition: "The technological and social prerequisites may have dramatically changed." For each dead company, ask:

- What technology exists now that didn't exist when they tried?
- What social behavior has changed? (Network effects, platform adoption, cultural shifts)
- What regulatory environment has shifted?
- What cost structures have collapsed? (AI inference costs, cloud compute, distribution costs)

Naval would frame this as: which leverage became permissionless since the last attempt? If the previous company needed capital (permissioned leverage) to build what you can now build with code (permissionless leverage), the maze has changed. A door that was locked is now open.

### Step 4: Find Your Unique Path

This is where specific knowledge meets the maze. Your path is not the consensus path. Naval: "Escape competition through authenticity." Balaji: "Is their path through the maze different from the consensus? If everyone agrees this is the right path, it's probably too crowded."

The unique path comes from combining your specific knowledge with a non-obvious leverage type:

- Everyone in your space is using media leverage (podcasts, YouTube). You use code leverage (a tool, a platform, an API).
- Everyone is using capital leverage (raising money, running ads). You use media leverage (building an audience first, then monetizing).
- Everyone is building with labor (agencies, consultancies). You build a product that replaces the labor.

Your specific knowledge tells you which path is viable for you that isn't viable for someone without your particular combination of skills, experience, and obsessions.

### Step 5: Time Your Entry Using the Hype Cycle

Balaji connects the idea maze to the Gartner Hype Cycle: "Trigger event -> people get amped -> they try and find it's hard -> trough of disillusionment -> those who stick with it make things happen."

The best time to enter is the trough of disillusionment, when:
- Media coverage has turned negative
- Most startups in the space have failed
- Talent is available (because everyone else left)
- The technology is quietly maturing underneath

Naval would add: the trough is when you can acquire leverage cheaply. Code talent is available. Media attention is uncontested. Capital is scared (so bootstrapping with code + media beats waiting for permission from investors).

## Example: AI Writing Tools (2024-2026)

**Dead companies and failed attempts:**
- Jasper AI (raised $125M, revenue declined as ChatGPT commoditized the same output)
- Copy.ai, Writesonic, dozens of wrapper apps (built on OpenAI API, no defensibility)
- Failed because: no specific knowledge baked in, pure code leverage on a commodity API

**Technology shift:** Model costs collapsed 10-100x. Prompt engineering became trivial. The raw capability is now a commodity.

**The maze today:** Everyone who built "AI + generic writing" is dead or dying. The path that's open: AI + specific domain knowledge + media leverage. A writing tool that encodes a specific creator's methodology (not generic prompts) and is distributed through that creator's audience.

**Naval's framework applied:** The specific knowledge is the creator's body of work. The leverage is code (the tool) + media (the creator's audience). No permission needed from investors. No labor to manage.

**Balaji's framework applied:** The dead companies built for stated preference ("I want AI to write for me"). The expressed preference is different: people want AI that writes like a specific expert they trust. The gap between "AI writing" (stated) and "AI writing that sounds like Naval" (expressed) is the open door in the maze.

## The Diagnostic Checklist

Before committing to a leverage strategy, answer these:

1. Can I name 3+ companies that tried this before? Do I know specifically why each one died?
2. What technology has changed since their attempt? Is a locked door now open?
3. Does my specific knowledge give me a path through the maze that a generalist can't see?
4. Am I choosing permissionless leverage (code/media) over permissioned leverage (capital/labor) where possible?
5. Where is this space on the hype cycle? Am I entering in the trough or at the peak?
6. Is my path the consensus path or a contrarian one? If everyone agrees, I'm probably too late.

If you can answer all six with specificity, you have a real leverage thesis. If your answers are vague ("some companies tried it," "AI is better now," "I think I have an edge"), you're wandering the maze without a map.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should have:
- A specific knowledge statement (one sentence)
- A dead-company map with named failures and specific causes
- A technology shift analysis identifying which doors opened
- A leverage type selection justified by your specific knowledge
- A hype cycle placement with entry timing rationale
