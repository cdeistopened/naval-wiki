## When to Use

You understand Naval's four forms of leverage (labor, capital, code, media) and are trying to decide which to pursue. Or you have identified a leverage strategy and want to evaluate whether it will actually produce value worth capturing. Or you are building an offer and need to understand why certain leverage-backed offers can charge exponentially more than others.

Hormozi's Value Equation gives you a scoring system for any opportunity. Naval's leverage framework tells you which tools are available. Together, they answer: "Which leverage strategy produces the most value with the least friction?"

## The Two Frameworks

### Hormozi's Value Equation

Alex Hormozi defines value as a fraction with four variables:

```
              Dream Outcome  x  Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Value = ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
              Time Delay  x  Effort & Sacrifice
```

> "Dream outcome is what separates whether someone's even interested in your category of offer or not."
> — Alex Hormozi, "How To Craft A $100M Offer In 6 Minutes"

> "If someone were able to click a button on a website and immediately look at their stomach and have a six-pack, that would be incredibly valuable."
> — Alex Hormozi, "The $100M Offer Formula"

The four variables:
- **Dream Outcome** (maximize): The aspirational end-state the customer wants
- **Perceived Likelihood** (maximize): How confident they are they will get the result
- **Time Delay** (minimize): How long between purchase and result
- **Effort & Sacrifice** (minimize): How much the customer has to do

### Naval's Four Forms of Leverage

Naval's taxonomy:
1. **Labor** — people working for you (permissioned, oldest, worst)
2. **Capital** — money deployed (permissioned, scalable)
3. **Code** — products with zero marginal cost (permissionless)
4. **Media** — audience with zero marginal cost (permissionless)

> "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people and products with no marginal costs of replication."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

## The Convergence: Scoring Leverage Through the Value Equation

Here is the insight that neither framework provides alone: you can score each form of leverage against the Value Equation to understand why certain leverage strategies dominate.

### Labor Leverage Through the Value Equation

| Variable | Score | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| Dream Outcome | Medium | Limited by what humans can do. Massage, custom cooking, personal training. |
| Perceived Likelihood | Medium-High | Humans can adapt to specific needs. Customization increases confidence. |
| Time Delay | High (bad) | Humans are slow. Training takes months. Delivery is linear. |
| Effort & Sacrifice | High (bad) | Managing people is effort-intensive. The customer often must coordinate, communicate, and wait. |

**Overall value score: Low-Medium.** Labor leverage produces decent outcomes but with high time delay and high effort. This is why labor-backed services (consulting, agencies, coaching) struggle to charge exponential premiums. The denominator is large.

Naval confirms this intuition: "Managing other people is incredibly messy. It requires tremendous leadership skills."

### Capital Leverage Through the Value Equation

| Variable | Score | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| Dream Outcome | High | Money produces money. The dream outcome (wealth) is clear. |
| Perceived Likelihood | Variable | Depends entirely on track record and judgment. Warren Buffett: very high. Unknown fund manager: low. |
| Time Delay | Medium-High | Capital compounds over time. Most investors wait years for returns. |
| Effort & Sacrifice | Low | The investor does little physical work once capital is deployed. |

**Overall value score: Medium-High for the investor, but hard to access.** Capital leverage scores well on effort (the customer/investor does not work hard) but poorly on time delay (compounding takes years). And it is permissioned — someone must trust you with their money first.

### Code Leverage Through the Value Equation

| Variable | Score | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| Dream Outcome | High | Software can transform workflows, create income, automate tedium. |
| Perceived Likelihood | High | SaaS products have demos, trials, reviews. Proof is visible before purchase. |
| Time Delay | Very Low | Software works immediately. Click, install, use. |
| Effort & Sacrifice | Very Low | Well-designed software reduces effort to near zero. |

**Overall value score: Very High.** Code leverage scores excellently on all four variables. The dream outcome is clear and specific. The likelihood is high (you can try before you buy). The time delay is near-zero (the software works immediately). The effort is near-zero (the software does the work for you).

This is why software companies can charge recurring fees for decades. They are selling Hormozi's "perfect ideal" — the instant, effortless result.

> "An army of robots is already here. It's very cheaply available. The bottleneck is just figuring out intelligent and interesting things to do to them."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

### Media Leverage Through the Value Equation

| Variable | Score | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| Dream Outcome | Variable | Depends on the content. Educational content: learn a skill. Entertainment: feel something. |
| Perceived Likelihood | Medium | Content is consumed before "purchase" (attention). The user evaluates in real-time. |
| Time Delay | Very Low | Content delivers value during consumption. Instant. |
| Effort & Sacrifice | Very Low | Consuming content is effortless. Reading, watching, listening. |

**Overall value score: High (but monetization is indirect).** Media scores extremely well on the denominator (zero time delay, zero effort). The challenge is that the numerator (dream outcome) is often fuzzy — what exactly does someone "get" from a podcast? The answer is knowledge, perspective, and connection to a thinker. Monetization happens through attention that converts to trust, which converts to purchases of other leverage-backed products.

Naval's own career demonstrates this. His podcast (media leverage) built the audience and reputation that attracted deal flow (capital leverage) and drove traffic to AngelList (code leverage).

## The Combined Insight

When you overlay the Value Equation on Naval's leverage taxonomy, a clear hierarchy emerges:

| Leverage Type | Value Equation Score | Naval's Ranking | Why They Agree |
|---------------|---------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Code | Highest | Permissionless, new fortunes | Minimizes denominator (time + effort), maximizes numerator (outcome + likelihood) |
| Media | High (indirect) | Permissionless, egalitarian | Near-zero denominator, variable numerator |
| Capital | Medium-High | Permissioned, scalable | Low effort but high time delay |
| Labor | Low-Medium | Permissioned, worst | High denominator on both time and effort |

The Value Equation mathematically confirms Naval's intuition. Code and media score highest because they minimize the denominator — they deliver value instantly with zero effort from the customer. Labor scores lowest because it maximizes the denominator — it requires management, time, and coordination.

## Practical Application: The Leverage Decision Matrix

When choosing your leverage strategy, score each option:

1. **What is the dream outcome for the person you serve?** Be specific.
2. **How can you increase their perceived likelihood of getting that outcome?** (Track record, guarantees, proof, demos)
3. **How fast can you deliver?** (Code = instant. Media = instant. Capital = years. Labor = weeks/months.)
4. **How much effort does the customer have to put in?** (Code = none. Media = minimal. Capital = minimal. Labor = significant coordination.)

The leverage strategy that scores highest on this matrix is your starting point.

## Example

A business consultant helps companies improve their sales processes. Today she does it through labor leverage: she goes on-site, trains the team, coaches managers. Her value equation scores:
- Dream Outcome: High (more sales)
- Perceived Likelihood: Medium (depends on her personal involvement)
- Time Delay: High (3-6 months to see results)
- Effort & Sacrifice: High (client team must change behaviors, attend trainings, implement)

She decides to encode her methodology into software (code leverage). The new value equation:
- Dream Outcome: Same (more sales)
- Perceived Likelihood: Higher (software has case studies, demos, measurable metrics built in)
- Time Delay: Very Low (software works from day one, starts tracking immediately)
- Effort & Sacrifice: Very Low (automated workflows, AI-suggested next steps, no training required)

The value equation jumped dramatically. She can now charge a premium SaaS fee to thousands of companies rather than a consulting fee to a dozen. And the product earns while she sleeps — the definition of Naval's wealth.
