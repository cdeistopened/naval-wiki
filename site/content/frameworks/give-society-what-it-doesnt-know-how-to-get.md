## When to Use

Use this when someone is choosing what to build. They want to create wealth but are stuck between ideas, or they are copying what already exists, or they are trying to make money by renting out their time in a slightly better way. They need a framework for identifying the kind of product that produces outsized returns.

This framework is Naval's core wealth-creation formula: find what society wants but cannot yet produce, then figure out how to deliver it at scale.

## The Framework

Naval explains the mechanism of wealth:

> "Money is IOUs from society saying, 'You did something good in the past. Now here's something that we owe you for the future.' And so society will pay you for creating things that it wants."

The key qualifier is "does not yet know how to get." If society already knows how to get it, it is already being produced at scale, and you are competing on efficiency — a low-leverage game. The high-leverage game is giving society something it wants but cannot yet produce:

> "If it did, they wouldn't need you. They would already be stamped out big time."

Naval redefines technology through this lens:

> "Almost everything that's in your house, in your workplace, and on the street used to be technology at one point in time. There was a time when oil was a technology, that made J.D. Rockefeller rich. There was a time when cars were technology, that made Henry Ford rich."

Technology is just the set of things that do not quite work yet. Once something works reliably, it stops being technology and becomes infrastructure. The wealth accrues to the people who figured it out while it was still technology.

The formula has two parts, and you need both:

**Part 1: Figure out what to provide.** This requires predicting what society will want, intersected with what is natural to you, within your capabilities and skillset.

**Part 2: Figure out how to scale it.** Building one is not enough. You need to build thousands, millions, or billions so everyone can have one.

> "Steve Jobs, and his team of course, figured out that society would want smartphones. A computer in their pocket that had all the phone capability times 100, and be easy to use. So, they figured out how to build that, and then they figured out how to scale it."

The prediction component is where judgment, specific knowledge, and taste intersect. You are not just building what people ask for. You are building what they will want before they know they want it.

## Example

In 2005, most people did not know they wanted a computer in their pocket. They had flip phones and were satisfied enough. Apple predicted the desire before it was articulated and then solved the engineering problem of delivering it at scale. The wealth was in the prediction plus the execution. A thousand companies could build phones. One company predicted the right phone and scaled it. That is the difference between commodity production and giving society what it does not know how to get.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Evaluate your current business idea against the two-part test: Does society want this but not know how to get it? Can I scale it?
2. Stop copying existing products and start looking for unmet, unarticulated demands
3. Identify the technology in your domain — the thing that does not quite work yet — and ask whether you are uniquely positioned to make it work
4. Combine prediction (what will society want?) with execution (how do I scale it?) rather than optimizing only one side
