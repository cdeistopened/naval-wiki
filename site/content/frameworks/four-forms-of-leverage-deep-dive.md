## When to Use

You want to understand why some people earn linearly and others earn exponentially. You are choosing between business models, career paths, or growth strategies and need a framework for evaluating which gives you the most output per unit of input. Or you are stuck at a certain level of income and cannot figure out why adding more hours does not produce more money.

This article is the foundational reference for the `leverage-stack-evaluator` skill. It covers Naval's complete taxonomy with every direct quote, his hierarchy, and the historical shift from permissioned to permissionless leverage.

## The Framework

### The Starting Point: Archimedes

Naval begins his leverage discussion with a quote he encountered as a young man:

> "Give me a lever long enough and a place to stand and I will move the Earth."
> — Archimedes, quoted by Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

We understand physical leverage intuitively. A seesaw. A crowbar. A pulley system. But Naval's argument is that our brains are not evolved to comprehend the scale of leverage available in modern society — and that the newest forms of leverage are the ones least understood.

> "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people and products with no marginal costs of replication."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

### Layer 1: Labor (People Working for You)

Labor is the oldest form of leverage. Instead of one person lifting rocks, ten people lift rocks. Everyone understands this because we evolved to understand it.

> "The oldest form of leverage is labor, which is people working for you. Instead of me lifting rocks, I can have 10 people lift rocks. Then just by my guidance on where the rock should go, a lot more rocks get moved than I could do myself. Everybody understands this because we're evolved to understand the labor form of leverage, so what happens is society overvalues labor as a form of leverage."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

Naval's verdict on labor is blunt: it is the worst form of leverage.

> "I would argue that this is the worst form of leverage that you could possibly use. Managing other people is incredibly messy. It requires tremendous leadership skills. You're one short hop from a mutiny or getting eaten or torn apart by the mob."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

Society overvalues it because our brains are wired for it. Parents are impressed when you manage a large team. Naive people measure your importance by your headcount.

> "This is why your parents are impressed when you get a promotion and you have lots of people working underneath you. This is why when a lot of naive people, when you tell them about your company, they'll say, 'How many people work there?' They'll use that as a way to establish credibility."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

The prescription is specific: you want the minimum amount of labor that enables you to access the other forms of leverage.

> "You really want to stay out of labor-based leverage. You want the minimum amount of people working with you that are going to allow you to use the other forms of leverage, which I would argue are much more interesting."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

**Key characteristics of labor leverage:**
- Permission required: YES — people must choose to follow you
- Scaling: Linear — 2x people does not equal 2x output
- Marginal cost: High — every person adds salary, benefits, management overhead, politics
- Historical role: Entire civilizations destroyed over the fight between capital and labor (Marxism)

### Layer 2: Capital (Money Working for You)

Capital is the second-oldest form of leverage. It dominated the 20th century — the era of bankers, fund managers, and financial asset managers.

> "Capital is a trickier form of leverage to use. It's more modern. It's the one that people have used to get fabulously wealthy in the last century. It's probably been the dominant form of leverage in the last century."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

Capital scales better than labor. Managing $10 billion is not 10x harder than managing $1 billion — the principles are the same, applied to larger numbers.

> "It scales very, very well. If you get good at managing capital, you can manage more and more capital much more easily than you can manage more and more people."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

But capital is permissioned. You need someone to give you money first. This creates a dependency.

> "If you have specific knowledge in a domain and if you're accountable and you have a good name in that domain, then people are going to give you capital as a form of leverage that you can use to then go get more capital."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Labor and Capital Are Old Leverage"

Naval acknowledges the psychological resistance to capital: "Deep down, I think we all dislike capital as a form of leverage because it feels unfair. It's this invisible thing that can be accumulated and passed across generations and suddenly seems to result in people having gargantuan amounts of money."

**Key characteristics of capital leverage:**
- Permission required: YES — someone must trust you with their money
- Scaling: Exponential when combined with judgment
- Marginal cost: Variable — capital can multiply or evaporate
- Entry requirement: Specific knowledge + accountability + track record

### Layer 3: Code (Products with Zero Marginal Cost)

This is where Naval's framework diverges from conventional wisdom. Code is the first of the two "new" forms of leverage.

> "The most interesting and the most important form of leverage is this idea of products that have no marginal cost of replication. This is the new form of leverage."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

The history is specific: "This was only invented in the last few hundred years. It got started with the printing press. It accelerated with broadcast media, and now it's really blown up with the Internet and with coding."

The robot army metaphor is Naval at his most vivid:

> "The robot army is already here. The robot revolution has already happened. We're about halfway through it. We're just adding in much more of the hardware component these days."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

> "Every great software developer, for example, now has an army of robots working for him at nighttime, while he or she sleeps, after they've written the code and it's just cranking away."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

> "An army of robots is already here. It's very cheaply available. The bottleneck is just figuring out intelligent and interesting things to do to them."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

**Key characteristics of code leverage:**
- Permission required: NO — permissionless
- Scaling: Exponential — one engineer can serve millions
- Marginal cost: Zero or near-zero per additional user
- Bottleneck: Figuring out what to tell the robots to do

### Layer 4: Media (Audience with Zero Marginal Cost)

Media is the second permissionless form of leverage. Naval's podcast itself is his example:

> "This podcast is a form of leverage. Long ago, I would have had to sit in a lecture hall and lecture each of you personally. I would have maybe reached a few hundred people and that would have been that."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

The gatekeepers are gone: "Then 40 years ago, 30 years ago, I would have to be lucky to get on TV, which is somebody else's leverage. They would have distorted the message. Today, thanks to the Internet, I can buy a cheap microphone, hook it up to a laptop or an iPad, and there you are all listening."

**Key characteristics of media leverage:**
- Permission required: NO — permissionless
- Scaling: Exponential — one piece of content can reach millions
- Marginal cost: Zero per additional viewer/reader/listener
- Modern examples: Joe Rogan ($50-100M/year from a podcast), PewDiePie (bigger than the news)

### The Permissioned vs. Permissionless Distinction

This is Naval's most important structural insight about leverage.

> "Probably the most interesting thing to keep in mind about the new forms of leverage is they are permissionless. They don't require somebody else's permission for you to use them or succeed."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

> "Coding, writing books, recording podcasts, tweeting, YouTubing, these kinds of things, these are permissionless. You don't need anyone's permission to do them, and that's why they are very egalitarian. They're great equalizers of leverage."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

The taxonomy:

| Form | Permission Required | Who Had It Before | Who Has It Now |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Labor | YES — people must follow you | Leaders, generals, managers | Same |
| Capital | YES — someone must fund you | Bankers, wealthy families | Same (but crowdfunding is changing this) |
| Code | NO | Didn't exist before computers | Anyone who can code |
| Media | NO | TV networks, publishers, newspapers | Anyone with a phone |

### The Magic Combination

Naval argues that the greatest wealth creation happens when you combine all forms of leverage:

> "Now, the beauty is when you combine all of these three. That's where tech startups really excel, where you take just the minimum, but highest output labor that you can get, which are engineers, and designers, product developers. Then you add in capital. You use that for marketing, advertising, scaling. You add in lots of code and media and podcasts and content to get it all out there."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

> "That is a magic combination, and that's why you see technology startups explode out of nowhere, use massive leverage and just make huge outsize returns."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

### The Hierarchy: Why New Fortunes Come from Code and Media

> "This newest form of leverage is where all the new fortunes are made, all the new billionaires. The last generation, fortunes were made by capital. That was the Warren Buffetts of the world. But the new generation's fortunes are all made through code or media."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product and Media are New Leverage"

### The Egalitarian Argument

Naval makes an ethical case for code and media leverage that is distinct from the economic case:

> "If you care about ethics in wealth creation, it is better to create your wealth using code and media as leverage because then those products are equally available to everybody as opposed to trying to create your wealth through labor or capital."
> — Naval Ravikant, "Product Leverage is Egalitarian"

The argument: Jeff Bezos doesn't get a better Google than you do. He doesn't watch better movies on Netflix. Code and media products converge toward equality because the marginal cost of serving one more person is zero. Labor-based products (a personal chef, a private massage) remain unequal because they scale linearly.

### The Strategic Sequence

Start with permissionless leverage (code or media) to build a track record. Use that track record to attract permissioned leverage (capital and talent). This is exactly how modern tech companies are built: founder writes code or builds an audience, attracts investors, then hires a team.

## Example

A marketing consultant charges $200/hour. She has labor leverage over her two junior employees, but her income is still time-bound. She decides to write a book distilling her methodology (media leverage — permissionless). The book sells 10,000 copies. A SaaS company approaches her to build her methodology into software (code leverage — permissionless). The software generates $50K/month in recurring revenue. A venture firm sees the traction and offers $2M in funding (capital leverage — now earned through permissionless leverage). She hires five engineers to scale the product (labor leverage — now justified by the other three forms).

In two years, she went from selling hours at $200 to owning a product that earns while she sleeps. The leverage stack: media attracted code, code attracted capital, capital funded minimal labor. The sequence matters. Starting with labor (hiring people) would have just made her a more stressed consultant.

5. Evaluate any business or career path by its leverage profile
