## When to Use

Use this when someone is caught in a cycle of compulsive pleasure-seeking and knows it. They scroll for hours, drink more than they intend to, eat food that makes them feel terrible, or consume news that makes them anxious. They are aware these behaviors are harmful but feel powerless against them. They may have tried willpower, apps, accountability partners, and habit trackers.

This framework explains why modern addictions are qualitatively different from historical temptations and why the struggle is structural, not personal.

## The Framework

Naval traces the problem to abundance itself, upgrading a line from the legendary Japanese swordsman Miyamoto Musashi:

> "In an age of abundance, pursuing pleasure for its own sake creates addiction."

Musashi's original advice — "Do not seek pleasure for its own sake" — was given in a world where pleasure was scarce and hard-won. You had to climb trees for fruit, socialize in person, hunt for food. The effort itself was a natural governor.

Today, every pleasure has been weaponized and made frictionless. The governing mechanisms have been removed:

> "He didn't have unlimited processed food, Internet pornography, marijuana and alcohol available on demand."

Naval names the core problem: addictions are counterfeit versions of real human activities.

> "Addictions let you engage in fake play and fake work. Before, you had to go socialize with friends; now, you can just get drunk with a bunch of strangers. Before, you had to go find a mate, create children and raise a family; now, you can just watch a lot of porn. Before, you had to hunt and climb trees to get fruit for a little bit of natural sweetness; now, you can buy all the gelato you want."

Each addiction substitutes a low-effort simulation for a high-effort real activity. Social media is fake socializing. Porn is fake intimacy. Processed food is fake nutrition. News is fake information-gathering. Video games are fake achievement.

The modern struggle is standing up to these weaponized substitutes:

> "The modern struggle is really about individuals—disconnected from their tribe, religion and cultural networks—who are trying to stand up to all these addictions that have been weaponized."

The mechanism of harm is desensitization. Small doses of easy pleasure reduce your capacity to enjoy harder, more real pleasures, while simultaneously creating dependence on the easy version.

## Example

A remote worker spends his evenings scrolling Twitter, eating delivery food, and watching YouTube until 1 AM. He knows he should exercise, cook, and read, but those feel effortful compared to the frictionless alternatives. The framework tells him: you are not lazy. You are fighting weaponized superstimuli that your ancestors never encountered. The first step is recognizing that the easy pleasures are fake versions of real activities. The second step is making the fake versions less accessible (delete apps, stop ordering delivery) so the real versions can compete again.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Name your addictions honestly by identifying which real activity each one counterfeits
2. Recognize that the difficulty of quitting is structural (weaponized abundance), not a personal moral failure
3. Increase friction on fake pleasures and decrease friction on real ones
4. Understand that breaking addictions is the central challenge of modern life, not a side project
