## When to Use

Use this when someone is working 60-hour weeks at a steady pace and getting mediocre results, or when someone is sitting on an idea "waiting for the right time."
Naval's work pattern is the opposite of the hustle-culture marathon.
It is more like a predator's hunting pattern: intense bursts of effort when opportunity or inspiration strikes, followed by genuine rest.

## The Framework

Naval rejects the marathon model of work entirely.

> "It's more like a lion hunting and less like a marathoner running. You sprint, then you rest, you reassess, and then you sprint again." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

### Why Not the Marathon

The marathon model assumes that consistent, moderate effort over long periods produces the best results.
Naval argues this is false for creative and leveraged work.
In those domains, the quality of the work matters far more than the quantity of hours.
And quality comes from inspiration and intensity, not endurance.

### Inspiration Is Perishable

The critical insight:

> "Inspiration is perishable — act on it immediately." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

When you have a flash of insight, a burst of creative energy, or a clear vision of what to build -- act on it right now.
Do not file it away for Monday.
Do not put it on the backlog.
The energy that accompanies inspiration dissipates rapidly.
By the time you get around to it next week, the insight will feel flat and the motivation will be gone.

### The Uneven Schedule

Your work pattern should be uneven by design.
Some days you work 16 hours in a state of flow.
Other days you read, walk, think, and do nothing productive.
The average may be 4-6 hours of real work per day, but it is concentrated in the moments of highest inspiration and leverage.

Nobody is working 80 sustained hours of high-output intellectual labor.
What they are doing is 20 hours of real work and 60 hours of meetings, email, and performative busyness.
The lion pattern acknowledges this reality and optimizes for the 20 hours that matter.

### Rest Is Not Laziness

The rest period is not laziness. It is reassessment.
The lion does not just lie around randomly.
It is scanning the environment, conserving energy, and positioning itself for the next sprint.
Similarly, rest periods should involve reading, thinking, observing, and building mental models.

### The Cultural Resistance

Western work culture valorizes the appearance of busyness.
Sitting at a desk for 8 hours is "working."
Reading a book on a Tuesday afternoon is "slacking."
Naval inverts this: the person reading the book is preparing for a sprint.
The person sitting at the desk is performing labor, not creating value.

### Practical Implementation

Keep your schedule as empty as possible.
Do not fill rest periods with low-value meetings or busywork.
When you feel the pull of inspiration -- a design idea, a business insight, a piece of writing -- drop everything and pursue it immediately.
The cost of a missed sprint far exceeds the cost of a delayed email.

## Example

A product designer has a breakthrough idea for a new feature while showering on a Saturday morning.
Instead of "saving it for Monday," she sits down and sketches the entire wireframe in 3 hours.
She then takes Sunday completely off.
On Monday, she presents a polished concept that would have taken a week to develop through the normal meeting-heavy process.
Her sprint-and-rest pattern produced better work in less calendar time.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Stop trying to sustain marathon-intensity effort
- Act immediately when inspiration strikes, regardless of the day or time
- Design their schedule to protect rest periods as genuine recovery
- Distinguish between real high-output work and performative busyness
