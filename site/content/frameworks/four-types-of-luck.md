## When to Use

Use this when someone says "I just need to get lucky" or dismisses another person's success as pure luck.
Also use it when someone is grinding without direction and wondering why nothing is breaking their way.
The framework shows that luck is not a single phenomenon but four distinct types, only one of which is random.
The other three can be cultivated.

## The Framework

Naval adapted this taxonomy from Dr. James Austin (via Marc Andreessen's blog) and added his own framing on how the fourth type transcends luck entirely and becomes destiny.

> "A lot of people think making money is about luck. It's not. It's about becoming the kind of person that makes money." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-06-money-luck.md

Each type has a well-known cliche -- except the last.

**Type 1: Blind Luck.**
Pure chance. A tornado misses your house. You win the genetic lottery.
The cliche: "dumb luck." You cannot cultivate it. It is the only type that is truly random.

**Type 2: Hustle Luck.**
Luck that comes from motion, persistence, and energy.
You are running around creating lots of opportunities, mixing reagents in a petri dish to see what combines.
The cliche: "fortune favors the bold."

> "You're generating enough force and hustle and energy that luck will find you." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-06-money-luck.md

**Type 3: Preparation Luck.**
Luck that comes from skill and sensitivity.
You have become so good at spotting patterns in a field that you notice a lucky break others miss.
The cliche: "chance favors the prepared mind" (Pasteur).

**Type 4: Character Luck (Destiny).**
The rarest and most powerful.
You build a unique character, brand, and skill set such that specific opportunities come to you and only you.
There is no common cliche for this type, which Naval sees as significant.

> "You build a unique character, a unique brand, a unique mindset, where then luck finds you." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-06-money-luck.md

Naval's deep-sea diver example: if you are the best deep-sea diver in the world, known for taking dives nobody else will attempt, and someone finds a sunken treasure ship, they come to you.
Their blind luck becomes your destiny luck.

> "At that point, it starts becoming so deterministic that it stops being luck. So, the definition starts fading from luck to more destiny." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-07-luck-destiny.md

Warren Buffett operates at Type 4.
He gets offered deals, gets to buy companies, gets to buy warrants and bail out banks at terms no one else can negotiate.
His reputation and character attract these opportunities.

> "Your character, your reputation, these are things that you can build that then will let you take advantage of opportunities that other people may characterize as lucky but you know that it wasn't luck." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-07-luck-destiny.md

As Benjamin Disraeli put it: "We make our fortunes and we call them fate."

The acid test:

> "In 1,000 parallel universes, you want to be wealthy in 999 of them. You don't want to be wealthy in the 50 of them where you got lucky. We want to factor luck out of it." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-06-money-luck.md

Naval applies this to his own life: he believes that if he lost all his money and were dropped on a random street in any English-speaking country, he would be wealthy again within 5-10 years.
That conviction is the mark of someone operating at Type 4.

The progression also has a compounding effect.
As you move from Type 1 toward Type 4, you "run out of unluck."
You neutralize the randomness, and your own talents and positioning become the dominant factor.

## Example

A software engineer learns machine learning as a hobby, builds open-source tools, and writes about niche applications in supply-chain optimization.
Three years later, a logistics company finds a critical bottleneck that only someone with her exact combination of ML expertise and supply-chain domain knowledge can solve.
They offer her equity to come aboard.
Onlookers call it luck.
She knows it is Type 4: her character attracted an opportunity that was invisible to everyone else.

Contrast this with her classmate who keeps waiting for "the right opportunity" to appear.
He is relying on Type 1 luck.
In 1,000 parallel universes, he is wealthy in maybe 10.
She is wealthy in 900.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Classify any recent "lucky" outcome into one of the four types
- Identify which type they are currently relying on most
- Design a concrete plan to move toward Type 3 and Type 4 luck
- Stop attributing others' success to blind luck when it is clearly destiny luck
- Apply the 1,000 parallel universes test to their own positioning
