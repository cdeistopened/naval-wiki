## When to Use

Use this when someone is evaluating career paths and trying to understand why some professions create millionaires while others cap out at a comfortable salary.
Also use it when someone feels stuck despite working long hours.
The core problem is almost always that their inputs (time, effort) are tightly coupled to their outputs (income, impact).
This framework explains why that coupling is the enemy of wealth and how to escape it.

## The Framework

Naval's key insight: you want a career where inputs and outputs are highly disconnected.
The more disconnected they are, the more room there is for creativity and leverage to multiply your results.

> "You really just want a job, or a career, or a profession where your inputs don't match your outputs." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

When inputs and outputs are tightly matched, you are essentially paid by the hour.
Whether you are a barista or a $900/hour lawyer, the structure is the same: work one hour, get paid for one hour.
Stop working, stop earning. This is a linear function.
There is no room for outlier results.

### The Lumberjack End of the Spectrum

> "If you're a lumberjack, even the best lumberjack in the world, assuming you're not working with tools, so the inputs and outputs are clearly connected. You're just using an ax, or a saw. You know, the best lumberjack in the world may be like 3x better than one of the worst lumberjacks." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

A 3x spread.
The best lumberjack is three times more productive than the worst.
There is almost no room for leverage.
The ceiling is physical.

### The Programmer End of the Spectrum

> "One great engineer can for example create bitcoin, and create billions of dollars worth of value. And an engineer who is working on the wrong thing, or not quite as good, or just not as creative, or thoughtful, or whatever, can work for an entire year, and every piece of code they ship ends up not getting used. Customers don't want it." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

The spread is not 3x but 1,000x or more.
Satoshi Nakamoto wrote code that created hundreds of billions of dollars in value.
Another engineer at the same skill level, working on the wrong problem, produced nothing.
Same inputs. Radically different outputs.
That gap is where wealth lives.

### Where Wealth Lives

The disconnection between input and output is what allows a single decision, a single insight, or a single product to generate outsized returns.
It is also what makes the outcome uncertain: you might work 1,000 hours for nothing, or 10 hours and change an industry.

> "Businesses that have high creativity and high leverage tend to be ones where you could do an hour of work, and it can have a huge effect. Or you can do 1,000 hours of work, and it can have no effect." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

This principle determines which professions can produce wealth:

| Tight coupling (low wealth potential) | Loose coupling (high wealth potential) |
|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Hourly labor | Software engineering |
| Retail clerk | Investing |
| Assembly line worker | Writing / media creation |
| Most salaried jobs | Entrepreneurship |
| Hourly consulting | Product design with distribution |

The pattern: creativity and leverage create the disconnection.
If your work can be replicated at zero marginal cost (code, media, design), or if a single decision can redirect millions of dollars (investing, executive leadership), your inputs and outputs are disconnected.

The implication for career planning is profound: do not ask "how can I work harder?"
Ask "how can I move into a domain where one great decision or one great product can produce 1,000x the result of average work?"

Even within a single profession, you can increase the disconnection.
A doctor who practices medicine has tight coupling.
A doctor who develops a medical device and licenses it has loose coupling.
Same knowledge base, different leverage.

## Example

Two friends graduate from the same university.
One becomes a dentist, the other a mobile app developer.
The dentist earns well but is paid per patient seen per hour.
Work 40 hours, earn X. Work 80 hours, earn 2X. Tight coupling.
The developer builds an app that gets 100,000 downloads.
The same hour of code serves 100,000 people simultaneously.
One has a 1:1 input-output ratio. The other has a 1:100,000 ratio.

Ten years later, the dentist has earned well but linearly.
The developer either has a failed app (zero return) or a massive hit (1,000x return).
The uncertainty is the price of disconnection, but the upside is asymmetric.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Evaluate the input-output coupling of their current career
- Identify whether they are in a 3x profession or a 1,000x profession
- Steer toward work where creativity and leverage disconnect inputs from outputs
- Recognize that "working harder" only helps in loosely-coupled professions
- Find ways to increase disconnection within their existing domain
