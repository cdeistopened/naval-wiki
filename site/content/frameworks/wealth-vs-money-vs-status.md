## When to Use

Use this when someone says they want to "make money" without distinguishing what they actually mean.
Most people collapse three separate concepts into one word.
A salaried employee earning $300K thinks they are wealthy.
A Twitter personality with 500K followers thinks their status is wealth.
A founder sitting on illiquid equity wonders why they feel broke.
Each is confused about a different thing.
This framework untangles the three.

Also use it when someone expresses guilt about wanting wealth, or when they attack wealth creators as greedy.
That reaction almost always comes from confusing wealth with status.

## The Framework

Naval draws a hard line between three things most people treat as synonyms.

### Wealth

Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep.
A factory. A rental property. A piece of software running on a server.
A business with employees who generate revenue whether you show up or not.

> "Wealth is the thing that you want. Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep. Wealth is the factory, the robots, that's cranking out things. Wealth is the computer program that's running at night, that's serving other customers." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

The purpose of wealth is not consumption. It is freedom.

> "The purpose of wealth is freedom. It's nothing more than that. It's not to buy fur coats, or drive Ferraris, or sail yachts, or jet around the world in your Gulfstream. That stuff gets really boring and really stupid, really fast. It's really so that you are your own sovereign individual." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

Wealth is what lets you wake up without an alarm, work on problems that matter to you, and never wear a tie like a collar around your neck.
The Gulfstream is a side effect.
The sovereignty is the point.

### Money

Money is IOUs from society. Social credits.
A record-keeping system for debits and credits of value exchanged.

> "Money is how we transfer wealth. Money is social credits. It is the ability to have credits and debits of other people's time." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

When you create value, society issues you an IOU: "You did something good in the past. Here is something we owe you for the future."
That IOU is money.
It gets debased when governments print more IOUs, or when people renege on their debts.

Money is the measurement tool. Wealth is the thing being measured.
You can hold a lot of money and own no wealth (a high earner who spends everything).
You can own enormous wealth and hold little money (a founder before an IPO).

### Status

Status is your rank in the social hierarchy.

> "Status, on the other hand, is a zero-sum game. It's a very old game. We've been playing it since monkey tribes. It's hierarchical. Who's number one? Who's number two? Who's number three? And for number three to move to number two, number two has to move out of that slot." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

The critical distinction: wealth is positive-sum; status is zero-sum.

> "Wealth is a very positive sum game. We create things together." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

When you build a product that solves a problem, you create new value.
Everyone in the world can have a house.
The more houses built, the easier it becomes to build houses.
When you climb a status ladder, someone else falls a rung.

> "People creating wealth will always be attacked by people playing status games." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

The evolutionary explanation matters.
Hunter-gatherers lived in entirely status-based societies because you could not store anything.
Farming introduced wealth. The modern industrial economy is heavily wealth-based.
But the status instinct persists, hardwired from thousands of years of tribal hierarchy.

> "The problem is that to win at a status game, you have to put somebody else down. That's why you should avoid status games in your life because they make you into an angry combative person. You're always fighting to put other people down, to put yourself and the people you like up." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md

Politics is a status game.
Social media arguments are status games.
Even sports is a status game: for the winner to exist, a loser must exist.
These games have their place, but they do not create wealth.

## Example

A senior engineer earns $400K at a large tech company.
She has high money and moderate status.
But she owns no equity in anything that earns while she sleeps.
If she stops showing up, income stops. She has money, not wealth.

Her college friend started a SaaS company with 200 paying customers generating $30K/month.
He pays himself $80K.
He has less money but more wealth: an asset that earns while he sleeps, that he can sell, that compounds.

The engineer should ask: am I building wealth or collecting a salary?
The answer determines whether she retires at 45 or works until 65.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Correctly classify their current income as money (salary, fees) or wealth (equity, assets, recurring revenue)
- Identify when they are playing a status game instead of a wealth game
- Articulate why wealth creation is ethical and positive-sum, not zero-sum extraction
- Recognize status attacks on wealth creators for what they are
- Redefine their goal from "make more money" to "build assets that earn while I sleep"
