## When to Use

Use this when someone is doing their own accounting, mowing their own lawn, waiting in line for an hour to save $20, or deliberating whether to hire help.
The aspirational hourly rate is a decision-making tool that cuts through these choices instantly.
If the task costs less than your rate to outsource, outsource it. Period.

Also use it when someone undervalues their time by accepting low-impact meetings, doing busywork, or penny-pinching on things that cost time but save trivial amounts of money.

## The Framework

Naval sets a specific, deliberately absurd number as his aspirational hourly rate: $5,000 per hour.
The rate is not what someone currently earns.
It is what they aspire to be worth.
It must feel unreasonably high.

> "Set a very high aspirational hourly rate for yourself, and stick to it. It should seem and feel absurdly high. If it doesn't, it's not high enough." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-01-hourly-rate.md

### The Decision Filter

The rate functions as a filter for every time-allocation decision:

- **Should I do my own taxes?** An accountant costs $200/hour. My rate is $5,000/hour. Outsource.
- **Should I drive 30 minutes to save $50?** That is paying myself $100/hour. My rate is $5,000/hour. Not worth it.
- **Should I take this meeting?** If the expected value of the meeting is less than $5,000, skip it.
- **Should I wait in line for a refund?** If the refund is $30 and the wait is an hour, I just valued my time at $30/hour. Not acceptable.

### The Psychology

Most people do not value their time because they have never put a number on it.
Once you have a number, it becomes viscerally uncomfortable to spend time on low-value activities.

> "You can't penny-pinch your way to wealth." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-01-hourly-rate.md

The hourly rate also exposes a critical asymmetry: people are far more protective of their money than their time.
Someone will spend an hour comparison-shopping to save $30 on a gadget without realizing they just paid themselves $30/hour.
If their aspirational rate is $1,000/hour, they burned $970 in opportunity cost.

### Why Aspirational, Not Current

The rate needs to be aspirational because it shapes behavior toward the future.
If you currently earn $50/hour but set your aspirational rate at $1,000/hour, you will stop spending time on $50/hour tasks and start looking for $1,000/hour opportunities.
The rate pulls you forward.

### Universal Application

Naval applies this universally:

- If you can hire a cleaner for $25/hour, do it.
- If you can hire an assistant for $30/hour, do it.
- If you can pay someone to handle logistics, pay them.
- If you can pay for a service that saves you 2 hours a week, pay for it.

Every hour freed from a low-value task is an hour available for high-leverage work: building products, creating content, making decisions, cultivating relationships.

The counterargument people raise is "I can't afford to outsource."
Naval's response: you cannot afford not to.
If you spend 10 hours a week on tasks that could be outsourced for $25/hour ($250/week), and those 10 hours could instead be spent on work worth $500/hour, you are leaving $4,750 on the table every week.

There is a deeper psychological principle at work.
When you treat your time as valuable, you begin to behave as though it is valuable.
You stop saying yes to every request.
You stop doing busywork.
You start asking "is this the highest and best use of my time right now?"
That question, asked consistently, reshapes your entire career trajectory.

## Example

A freelance consultant charges clients $150/hour.
She sets an aspirational rate of $2,000/hour.
She immediately notices she spends 8 hours per week on invoicing, scheduling, and email management.
She hires a virtual assistant for $25/hour (10 hours/week = $250/week).
Those 8 hours freed up let her build a productized service that eventually generates $10K/month in recurring revenue.
The $250/week investment returned $10K/month because she stopped doing $25/hour work and started doing $2,000/hour work.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Set a specific aspirational hourly rate (it should feel absurdly high)
- Audit their week and identify every activity below that rate
- Make immediate outsourcing decisions based on the rate comparison
- Stop penny-pinching on money and start protecting their time
- Apply the rate to personal decisions, not just work decisions
