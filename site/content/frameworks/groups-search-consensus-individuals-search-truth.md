## When to Use

Use this when someone is living according to society's script while privately knowing it is wrong for them. They are staying in a career because it is respectable, maintaining relationships because they are expected, or holding opinions because they are popular. They feel guilt when they deviate from the consensus and cannot tell whether the guilt is legitimate or programmed.

This framework separates social truths from individual truths and names the mechanism of social control.

## The Framework

Naval draws a hard line between what groups do and what individuals do:

> "Individuals search for truth but groups search for consensus—and society is the largest group. So the biggest problem we run into is this: What society wants for you is not always what's good for you."

Society operates on shared fictions. These fictions are useful — they allow cooperation, prevent violence, and maintain order. But they come at a cost, and the cost is borne by the individual:

> "Society is a set of collective lies that we all believe so we can get along. It allows us to establish a lower common denominator consensus so we don't all kill each other and we can cooperate."

Naval gives specific examples. "Money won't make you happy" is a social truth designed to prevent envy and maintain cohesion. But individuals chasing money know it can remove many sources of unhappiness. The social truth and the individual truth diverge.

School is another example. Society says children go to school for education. In reality, they get about an hour of education per day. The rest is socialization, compliance training, and babysitting. This is useful for society but often harmful for the individual child.

The most potent mechanism of social control is not punishment. It is guilt:

> "Guilt is society programming you so effectively that you become your own warden."

You do not need an external enforcer when you have internalized the rules so deeply that you punish yourself for violations. The voice telling you to feel guilty about leaving a stable job, or not wanting children, or disagreeing with your peer group — that voice may not be yours. It may be society's voice, installed so early that you cannot distinguish it from your own.

> "Truth-seeking is a hard business. You essentially have to understand, with deep conviction, things that you've been programmed to misunderstand."

## Example

A lawyer at a prestigious firm realizes he hates the work. His parents are proud. His friends are envious. His salary is excellent. When he considers leaving, he feels crushing guilt — as though he would be wasting his education, disappointing his family, and throwing away something valuable. The framework asks: whose voice is producing that guilt? Is it his own considered judgment, or is it the consensus speaking through him? If the guilt dissolves when he honestly examines it, it was programming. If it remains after honest examination, it may be genuine.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Distinguish between social truths (useful for group cohesion) and individual truths (useful for your life)
2. Identify guilt as a potential artifact of social programming rather than an automatic moral signal
3. Name the specific consensus you are conforming to and ask whether it serves you
4. Accept the cost of truth-seeking: you may end up at odds with your group
