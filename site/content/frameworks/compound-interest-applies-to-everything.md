## When to Use

Use this when someone is frustrated that years of effort have not paid off, or when someone is considering abandoning a long-term game for a short-term win.
Also use it when someone is switching careers, relationships, or industries every 2-3 years and wondering why nothing compounds.
The framework explains why all returns in life -- not just financial -- follow the compound interest curve, and why most people quit right before the curve inflects.

## The Framework

Naval extends the compound interest concept far beyond money.

> "All the benefits in life come from compound interest. Whether it's in relationships, or making money, or in learning." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-long-term.md

This is why Naval's central directive is to play long-term games with long-term people.

> "Play long-term games with long-term people. All returns in life, whether in wealth, relationships, or knowledge, come from compound interest." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-long-term.md

### In Relationships

When you have worked with someone for 20 years, trust compounds to the point where you can do deals on a handshake.
You skip the lawyers, skip the due diligence, skip the posturing.
That trust is a compounded asset.

### In Knowledge

Reading in the same domain for a decade creates pattern recognition that a newcomer cannot match.
Each new piece of information connects to a dense web of existing knowledge, making learning faster and insights deeper.

### In Reputation

Each good decision you make in public adds to a track record.
After enough decisions, people seek you out for deals, advice, and partnerships.
Reputation compounds quietly until it reaches escape velocity.

### In Business

The most successful businesses are ones that have been compounding for decades.
Switching industries resets the compound clock to zero.

> "When you switch industries, you're starting over from scratch. You're not going to have the relationships, the knowledge, the expertise. It takes many, many years to build." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-long-term.md

### The Game Theory Dimension

In long-term games, cooperation dominates. In short-term games, defection dominates.

> "In a long-term game, everybody is making each other rich. In a short-term game, everybody is making themselves rich." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-long-term.md

The iterated prisoner's dilemma explains why: when you know you will interact with someone again and again, the optimal strategy is cooperation.
Trust builds. Both sides benefit.
In one-shot games, there is no incentive to cooperate.

### The Effort Paradox

Naval observes that the effort required to build a small business is nearly the same as the effort to build a large one:

> "It takes just as much effort to create a small business as it does to create a large one. Whether you're Elon Musk or the guy running three Italian restaurants in town, you're working 80 hours a week; you're sweating bullets; you're hiring and firing people." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-07-19-relationships.md

If the effort is the same, but the compounding potential is different, then choosing which game to play is the most important decision you make.

### The Non-Intuitive Property

Most of the benefits of compounding come at the end.
Year 1 through year 5 feel slow and unrewarding.
Year 15 through year 20 feel exponential.
This is why most people quit: they abandon the game before the curve inflects.

## Example

Two consultants start in the same niche at the same time.
Consultant A stays in B2B SaaS for 15 years, building deep relationships with 200 companies and a reputation as the go-to expert.
Consultant B pivots every 3 years: SaaS, then crypto, then AI, then climate tech.
After 15 years, Consultant A gets inbound deal flow, premium pricing, and referrals without marketing.
Consultant B is still introducing himself, still building credibility from scratch, still competing on price.
The compounding gap is enormous.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Identify which long-term games they are playing (and which they keep resetting)
- Recognize the compound interest curve in their own career
- Evaluate relationships as compounding or non-compounding
- Stop switching industries, partners, or strategies before the returns arrive
