## When to Use

Use this when someone brags about the size of their team, when a founder is hiring before they have product-market fit, or when someone evaluates businesses by headcount.
Society has a deep bias toward labor as leverage because it is the oldest form and the most visible.
Naval argues it is also the worst form.
This framework explains why.

## The Framework

There are three forms of leverage: labor (people), capital (money), and products with zero marginal cost of replication (code and media).
Most people intuitively respect the first and are impressed by the second.
They underestimate the third.

> "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people and products with no marginal costs of replication." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-15-labor-capital.md

### Why Labor Is the Worst Leverage

Labor is the oldest form. Kings, pharaohs, and generals built empires by commanding armies.
In business, the legacy of this is that people are impressed when you say "I manage 500 people."
It signals power.

But Naval is clear:

> "Managing other people is incredibly messy. It requires tremendous leadership skills. You're one short hop from a mutiny or getting eaten or getting thrown out of the bed." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-15-labor-capital.md

Every person you manage adds complexity: egos, politics, communication overhead, HR issues, misalignment.
The overhead is not linear.
A team of 10 does not require 2x the management of a team of 5.
It requires 5x or more because of the combinatorial explosion of relationships.

### The Leverage Hierarchy

Naval's preferred ranking:

1. **Code** -- software that works while you sleep, zero marginal cost to replicate, permissionless
2. **Media** -- content that reaches millions at zero marginal cost, permissionless
3. **Capital** -- money deployed at scale, but requires permission
4. **Labor** -- people working for you, requires permission, highest overhead

> "Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich. You can create software and media that works for you while you sleep." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-17-product-media.md

> "An army of robots is already here. It's very cheaply available. The bottleneck is just figuring out intelligent and interesting things to do to them." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-17-product-media.md

### The Historical Bias

For most of human civilization, labor was the only form of leverage available.
Kings were powerful because they commanded armies.
Industrialists were powerful because they employed thousands.
The social signal "I have many people working for me" was a reliable proxy for power for 10,000 years.

That proxy broke in the last 30 years.
The most valuable companies in the world have remarkably few employees relative to their market capitalization.
Revenue per employee at top tech companies exceeds $1M.
At a traditional services firm, it might be $100K.
The difference is entirely leverage: tech deploys code that serves billions.

The ideal modern company maximizes code and media leverage while minimizing labor.
A one-person SaaS company with 10,000 customers has more leverage than a 50-person services firm with 20 clients.

## Example

Two founders build competing businesses in the same market.
Founder A hires a team of 30 sales reps, 10 engineers, and 5 managers.
Founder B writes a product that sells itself through content marketing and a self-serve funnel.
She has 4 employees.
At $5M in revenue, Founder A's company has razor-thin margins because payroll eats everything.
Founder B's company runs at 80% margins.
When the market contracts, Founder A lays off 20 people.
Founder B turns off a few ad campaigns.
Labor leverage is fragile. Code leverage is resilient.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Stop equating headcount with success
- Evaluate whether they need to hire or whether they need better code/media leverage
- Recognize that "I manage 100 people" is an overhead burden, not an achievement
- Prioritize permissionless leverage (code and media) over permissioned leverage (labor and capital)
