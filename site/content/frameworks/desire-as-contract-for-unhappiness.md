## When to Use

Use this when someone is grinding toward a goal while being miserable along the way, telling themselves it will all be worth it when they arrive. Or when someone has achieved something significant and is already fixated on the next thing, wondering why the satisfaction evaporated so quickly. They are on the hedonic treadmill and do not know it.

This framework makes the cost of desire explicit before you sign the contract.

## The Framework

Naval states the mechanism plainly:

> "Desire is a contract you make to be unhappy until you get what you want. You become disturbed because you want something. Then you work really hard to get it and are miserable in the meantime. Finally, when you get it, you revert to the state you were in before you had it."

This is the full cycle. Desire creates disturbance. Pursuit creates misery. Attainment creates brief relief. Then the baseline returns and a new desire appears.

The key insight is that attainment does not produce a permanent upgrade:

> "It's not like you achieve some peak level of bliss that you stay on forever."

If obtaining things made us permanently happy, then cavemen would have been the most miserable humans in history, and we, surrounded by abundance, would be ecstatic. Yet net happiness per person is flat or declining. Modernity has multiplied desires faster than it has satisfied them.

Naval redefines happiness not as getting what you want but as the absence of wanting:

> "Happiness is returning to the state where nothing is missing in this moment."

This does not mean you stop working or pursuing goals. It means you become conscious of the contract you are signing each time you pick up a new desire. You can still sign it. But you sign it with open eyes, knowing the terms: unhappiness now, brief relief later, then back to baseline.

The practical move is to reduce the number of active desires. Not to zero — that is renunciation. But to a small, carefully chosen set where the pursuit itself is enjoyable, not just the destination.

## Example

An engineer wants to become a VP of Engineering. He spends four years grinding, sacrificing weekends, taking on thankless projects, managing politics. He gets the title. For two weeks he feels great. Then he starts thinking about becoming a CTO. The framework does not say he should not have pursued the VP role. It says he should have noticed the contract: four years of voluntary unhappiness in exchange for two weeks of relief. If the work itself was not satisfying, the deal was bad.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Identify your active desires and recognize each one as a signed contract for present unhappiness
2. Ask of each desire: "Is the pursuit itself enjoyable, or am I only tolerating it for the destination?"
3. Consciously reduce the number of concurrent desires to a small set
4. Recognize the moment of attainment for what it is: a return to baseline, not an arrival at bliss
