## When to Use

Use this when someone says "I'm interested in X but there's no money in it" or "my skills are too specific to find a market."
Before the internet, that might have been true.
If you were the world's best underwater basket weaver in a small town, your market was zero.
Now, with internet reach, the set of possible careers has expanded by orders of magnitude.
This framework explains why obscure interests can become viable businesses.

## The Framework

Naval's observation is structural, not motivational.
The internet did not just create new jobs.
It expanded the dimensionality of the career space itself.

> "The internet has massively broadened the possible space of careers. Most people haven't figured this out yet." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-possible-careers.md

Before the internet, your career options were constrained by local demand.
You could be a doctor, lawyer, teacher, farmer, or shopkeeper.
Your market was whoever lived within driving distance.
Unusual skill combinations had no audience.

> "The internet allows you to scale any niche obsession." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-possible-careers.md

Now, a person with a unique combination of interests -- say, vintage synthesizer repair and YouTube education -- can reach every vintage synthesizer owner on the planet.
The niche is tiny in any single city but enormous globally.

### The Mechanism

The internet lets you find your 1,000 true fans (Kevin Kelly's concept) no matter how specific your offering.
You do not need mass appeal.
You need deep appeal to a specific group that is now reachable.

> "The internet connects everyone. It connects all the buyers to all the sellers. So you can be the best person at something that's a weird combination, and the internet will make it possible for you to find all the other people who want what you have." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-19-possible-careers.md

Naval links this to the specific knowledge framework.
Your unique combination of skills, interests, and experiences -- the things that feel like play to you -- are precisely the things the internet can monetize.
Before, there was no distribution channel for unusual combinations.
Now there is.

### The Career Planning Implication

Career planning should not start with "what jobs are available" but with "what am I uniquely good at, and who in the world wants that?"
The internet collapses the distribution problem.
Stop trying to fit into pre-existing job descriptions.
Create a role that only you can fill, and use the internet to find the market for it.

### The Expanding Space

Before the internet, there were perhaps 1,000 well-known career paths.
Now there are millions of viable micro-careers, each one a unique combination of skills and interests served by internet distribution.
The person who teaches sourdough baking to celiac sufferers.
The consultant who helps dentists optimize their Google Ads.
The programmer who builds tools for indie musicians.
None of these careers existed 20 years ago. All are viable today.

The internet also reduces the cost of experimentation.
You can test whether your niche has a market before committing to it.
Write a newsletter. Record a podcast. Build a small tool.
If 100 people show up, you have something. If zero do, you iterate.
The cost of this testing was prohibitive before the internet. Now it is nearly free.

## Example

A former chef with a computer science degree starts writing about the intersection of food science and machine learning.
In her city, there is zero demand for this combination.
Online, she finds a community of food-tech startups, food scientists, and ML engineers who subscribe to her newsletter, hire her for consulting, and invite her to speak at conferences.
Her niche obsession, unreachable before the internet, now generates $200K/year.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Identify their unique combination of skills and interests (the weirder, the better)
- Stop constraining their career vision to local or conventional job markets
- Use the internet to test whether a niche obsession has a viable global audience
- Reframe "I'm too niche" as "I have a defensible position that the internet can distribute"
