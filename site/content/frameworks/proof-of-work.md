## When to Use

Use this when you catch yourself trying to network your way to success. You are scheduling coffee meetings, attending conferences hoping for a lucky break, or cold-emailing people to "pick their brain." You feel busy but nothing is actually getting built. Your calendar is full of get-to-know-you calls, yet your portfolio of real work is empty.

This framework reorders the sequence. It says: build first, then the meetings come to you. The proof of work is what opens doors, not the handshake.

## The Framework

Naval keeps a famously clean calendar while staying relentlessly busy:

> "I keep a very clean calendar. I have almost no meetings on it. When some people see my calendar, they almost weep."

The principle is ruthless elimination of unearned access:

> "If someone wants a meeting, see if they will do a call instead. If they want to call, see if they will email instead. If they want to email, see if they will text instead."

But this is not about being antisocial. It is about the order of operations. Early in your career, exploring and meeting people makes sense. Later, when you are exploiting what you have built, meetings become a tax on your most valuable asset: unstructured time.

The alternative to networking is proof of work:

> "You have to do the work. To use a crypto analogy, you have to have proof of work."

When you have proof of work and something genuinely interesting, you put it in an email and send it. You do not need a meeting. The work speaks.

Naval connects this to his luck framework. Networking produces Type One luck (blind luck) and Type Two luck (hustle luck). But proof of work produces Type Three and Type Four luck, where your reputation and unique point of view attract opportunities:

> "A busy calendar and a busy mind will destroy your ability to do great things in this world. If you want to do great things—whether you're a musician or entrepreneur or investor—you need free time and a free mind."

## Example

A startup founder wants to raise a Series A. She could spend three months going to demo days, having coffee with investors, and attending networking dinners. Or she could spend those three months shipping product, growing users, and publishing her metrics publicly. The second path creates proof of work. When she finally emails investors, the email contains traction data, not a request to "get to know each other." The investor responds because the work is undeniable.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Audit your calendar and eliminate meetings that exist only for relationship-building with no proof of work behind them
2. Identify the one thing you could build or ship that would make meetings come to you
3. Replace "Can I pick your brain?" emails with "Here is what I built, here is the specific question" emails
4. Protect large blocks of unstructured time for actual creation
