## When to Use

Use this when someone earns a strong salary yet still feels financially trapped.
The feeling is correct: they are renting their time, and renters do not build equity.
Also use it when someone is deciding between a $200K salary and a startup with lower pay but equity.
The math of wealth creation runs through ownership, not income.

## The Framework

Naval treats this as one of the most important ideas in the entire tweetstorm.

> "You are not going to get rich renting out your time. You must own equity -- a piece of the business -- to gain your financial freedom." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

Three structural problems with renting time:

**1. Inputs match outputs.**
In any salaried role, you are paid roughly in proportion to the hours you put in.
When you sleep, you earn nothing. When you vacation, you earn nothing. When you retire, you earn nothing.

> "Your inputs are very closely tied to your outputs. In almost any salaried job, even at one that's paying a lot per hour like a lawyer, or a doctor, you're still putting in the hours, and every hour you get paid." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

**2. You are replaceable.**
A set role that can be taught can eventually be filled by someone cheaper, younger, or automated.

> "You're essentially replaceable because you're now doing a set role. Most set roles can be taught. If they can be taught like in a school, then eventually you're going to be competing with someone who's got more recent knowledge, who's been taught, and is coming in to replace you." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

**3. You are not being creative.**
When you rent time, you execute someone else's vision.
The intellectual property, the brand, and the upside all belong to the principal, not to you.

### Where Wealth Actually Comes From

> "Everybody who really makes money at some point owns a piece of a product, or a business, or some kind of IP. That can be through stock options, so you can be working at a tech company. That's a fine way to start. But usually the real wealth is created by starting your own companies, or by even investors." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

Even doctors who get truly rich do so by building a business, not by practicing medicine:

> "If you look at even doctors who get rich, like really rich, it's because they open a business. They open like a private practice. And that private practice builds a brand, and that brand attracts people. Or they build some kind of a medical device, or a procedure, or a process with an intellectual property." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-08-renting-time.md

The Nassim Taleb warning drives the point home:

> "The most dangerous things are heroin and a monthly salary." — Nassim Taleb, cited by Naval

A salary is dangerous because it is just comfortable enough to prevent you from building something you own.
It feels safe but creates dependency.
You get raises, promotions, and the feeling of progress, but you are building someone else's wealth.

### The Path from Renting to Owning

The transition is not necessarily quitting your job tomorrow.
Stock options at a startup are a form of equity.
Building a side project that generates revenue is equity.
Writing a book that sells copies while you sleep is equity.
Investing in index funds is equity (you own a piece of businesses).

The question is always: do I own something that works when I do not?

The critical mental shift is from "how much do I earn per hour" to "what do I own that generates income without my presence."
The first question measures money. The second measures wealth.

## Example

A marketing director at a Fortune 500 company earns $250K.
She works 50-hour weeks, manages a team, and executes campaigns.
If she leaves, the company replaces her within a month.
Her income stops the day she stops showing up. She is renting her time.

Her former colleague left to build a marketing automation tool.
He earns $90K in the first year but owns 60% of a company with $500K ARR.
His equity is worth multiples of his salary, and the software generates revenue whether he is awake or asleep.

Five years later, the director has earned $1.25M in salary and spent most of it.
The founder has a business worth $5M and growing.
The ownership gap has compounded.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Identify whether their current income comes from renting time or owning equity
- Recognize the three structural traps of time-renting
- Evaluate paths to equity: starting a company, joining early-stage with options, building products, investing
- Understand why a high salary can be a trap rather than a path to wealth
- Name one concrete step to move from renting to owning this quarter
