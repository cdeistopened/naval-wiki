### 1. The Specific Knowledge Identifier
- **What it does:** Helps someone discover what their specific knowledge actually is—the intersection of innate talent, obsessive curiosity, and market value that can't be trained or automated.
- **User input:** Their work history, hobbies, things that feel like play to them, what others say they're good at, what they'd do for free.
- **Output:** A ranked list of their specific knowledge candidates, scored against Naval's criteria (can't be trained, found by pursuing curiosity, feels like play, is at the edge of knowledge, is highly creative or technical).
- **Source-specific knowledge:** Naval's specific, multi-layered definition: "Specific knowledge is found much more by pursuing your innate talents, your genuine curiosity, and your passion. It's not by going to school for whatever is the hottest job." His mother-noticing-his-business-mind story. His distinction between timely specific knowledge (machine learning expertise) and timeless specific knowledge (persuasion). The key test: "Building specific knowledge will feel like play to you but look like work to others." The Scott Adams skill-stack combination insight but with Naval's warning not to be too deliberate: "If you go around trying to build it a little too deliberately, if you become too goal-oriented on the money, then you won't pick the right thing."

### 2. The Leverage Stack Evaluator
- **What it does:** Helps someone evaluate which form of leverage to pursue (labor, capital, code, or media) given their current position, skills, and goals.
- **User input:** Current role, specific knowledge, resources available, risk tolerance, whether they can code, whether they can communicate/sell.
- **Output:** A recommended leverage strategy with specific next steps, ordered from permissionless (start now) to permissioned (requires others' buy-in).
- **Source-specific knowledge:** Naval's four-layer leverage taxonomy is distinctive and original: (1) Labor—oldest, worst, "managing other people is incredibly messy"; (2) Capital—"dominant form in the last century," requires specific knowledge and accountability to obtain; (3) Code/Products—"zero marginal cost of replication," permissionless; (4) Media—permissionless, "the robot army is already here." The critical insight that code and media are *permissionless*: "You don't need anyone's permission to do them, and that's why they are very egalitarian." The magic combination is all three: minimum high-output labor + capital for scaling + code and media for reach.
- **Key sources:** `2019-04-15-labor-capital.md`, `2019-04-17-product-media.md`, `2019-04-19-egalitarian-leverage.md`, `2019-04-22-business-models.md`
- **Key quotes:**
  - "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people and products with no marginal costs of replication."
  - "Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich. You can create software and media that works for you while you sleep."
  - "An army of robots is already here. It's very cheaply available. The bottleneck is just figuring out intelligent and interesting things to do to them."

## FRAMEWORK ARTICLES (reference-only, no interactivity needed)

### 1. Wealth vs. Money vs. Status
The tripartite distinction: wealth = assets that earn while you sleep, money = IOUs/social credits, status = rank in social hierarchy. Wealth is positive-sum, status is zero-sum. Source: `2019-02-28-seek-wealth.md`

### 2. The Four Types of Luck
(1) Blind luck, (2) Hustle luck ("fortune favors the bold"), (3) Preparation luck ("chance favors the prepared mind"), (4) Character/destiny luck (no common cliche—you build unique character until opportunity finds you). Source: `2019-03-06-money-luck.md`, `2019-03-07-luck-destiny.md`

### 3. The Arm Yourself Sequence
Specific knowledge → accountability → leverage → judgment. The ordered progression for wealth creation. Source: `2019-03-25-specific-knowledge.md` and full tweetstorm series

### 4. Inputs vs. Outputs Disconnection
You want professions where inputs and outputs are highly disconnected. Creativity and leverage create the disconnection. A lumberjack is 3x; a great programmer is 1000x. Source: `2019-03-08-renting-time.md`

### 5. Renting Time vs. Owning Equity
You will not get rich renting out your time. You must own equity. When you're renting time, you're replaceable, inputs match outputs, and you're not being creative. Source: `2019-03-08-renting-time.md`

### 6. The Aspirational Hourly Rate
Set a rate so high it seems absurd ($5,000/hr was Naval's). If something can be outsourced for less than your rate, outsource it. "You can't penny-pinch your way to wealth." Source: `2019-05-01-hourly-rate.md`

### 7. Compound Interest Applies to Everything
Not just money—relationships, knowledge, health, reputation. "All the benefits in life come from compound interest." This is why you play long-term games. Source: `2019-03-19-long-term.md`

### 8. The Internet Broadens the Career Space
"The internet allows you to scale any niche obsession." Your unique combination of interests + internet reach = viable career. Pre-internet, you had to conform to local demand. Source: `2019-03-19-possible-careers.md`

### 9. Escape Competition Through Authenticity
"No one can compete with you on being you." Mimetic desire (Girard) drives you to compete. Authenticity gets you out. If you're building something that's an extension of who you are, competition is irrelevant. Source: `2019-05-16-competition-authenticity.md`

### 10. Society Overvalues Labor Leverage
People are impressed by headcount, but labor is the worst form of leverage. "Managing other people is incredibly messy." You want the minimum labor that lets you access code, media, and capital leverage. Source: `2019-04-15-labor-capital.md`

### 11. Permissionless vs. Permissioned Leverage
Labor and capital require someone's permission. Code and media don't. This is what makes the new forms of leverage egalitarian. Source: `2019-04-17-product-media.md`

### 12. The Maker-Taker Dynamic
Civilizations fall when takers outnumber makers. The parasite-host analogy. "Any organism can only withstand a small number of parasites. When the parasitic element gets too far out of control, you die." Source: `2019-03-03-ethical-wealth.md`, `2019-03-05-free-markets.md`

### 13. Impatience with Actions, Patience with Results
Act immediately on what's in your control; be patient with outcomes in complex systems. "If I discover a problem in one of my businesses, I won't sleep until the resolution is at least in motion." Source: `2019-05-06-work-hard.md`

### 14. The Sprint-Rest Work Pattern
"It's more like a lion hunting and less like a marathoner running." Nobody really works 80 hours at high output. Inspiration is perishable—act on it immediately. Source: `2019-05-06-work-hard.md`

### 15. Product-Market-Founder Fit
Andreessen's product-market fit expanded to three variables. What, who, and how hard. "If getting wealthy is your goal, you're going to have to work as hard as you can. But hard work is no substitute for who you work with and what you work on." Source: `2019-05-06-work-hard.md`

### 16. Be Too Busy to Do Coffee
Keep a clean calendar but be busy with high-impact work. Ruthlessly decline meetings. Downgrade: meeting → call → email → text → ignore. "A busy calendar and a busy mind will destroy your ability to do great things." Source: `2019-05-08-meetings.md`

### 17. Proof of Work
"People will meet with you when you have proof of work." Product progress is the entrepreneur's unfake-able resume. Don't network; build something first. Source: `2019-05-08-meetings.md`

### 18. Reject Most Advice
"The best founders I know read and listen to everyone. But then they ignore everyone and make up their own mind." Advice gives you anecdotes to recall when you get your own experience. "90% of my tweets are maxims that become mental hooks." Source: `2019-05-20-reject-advice.md`

### 19. A Calm Mind, a Fit Body, a House Full of Love
"These things cannot be bought—they must be earned." Money solves money problems but not these three. Source: `2019-05-21-finally-wealthy.md`

### 20. Happiness as Peace from Mind
"We say peace of mind, but what we really want is peace from mind." During moments of greatest pleasure, the mind goes quiet. The mind is a tool that became the master. Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 21. Desire as a Contract for Unhappiness
"Desire is a contract you make to be unhappy until you get what you want." You work hard, get it, revert to baseline. No single thing makes you permanently happy. Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 22. The Addiction Framework
"In an age of abundance, pursuing pleasure for its own sake creates addiction." Addictions are fake work and fake play. Breaking addiction requires a new lifestyle, not just willpower. Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 23. Stress as Inability to Decide What's Important
"In mental terms, stress is an inability to decide what's important. You want two incompatible things at once." When you give up on something, it's no longer stressful. Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 24. The Path to Peace Is Truth, Not Practice
"Self-improvement is just a dressed up form of self-conflict." Understanding is what changes you, not technique. When you see something clearly enough, bad habits dissolve on their own. Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 25. Groups Search for Consensus, Individuals Search for Truth
What society wants for you isn't what's good for you. Even smart people go along with society's lies. "Guilt is society programming you so effectively that you become your own warden." Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md`

### 26. Wisdom Begets Stoicism (Not the Reverse)
You don't become wise by being stoic—you become stoic by becoming wise. "The closer you are to the truth, the more silent you become inside." Source: `2021-03-12-happiness.md` (citing Kapil Gupta)

### 27. The Only True Test of Intelligence
"The only true test of intelligence is if you get what you want out of life." AI fails this test because it has no desires. Intelligence is measured in the arena—it's relative and adversarial. Source: `episodes/2026-02-ai/POLISHED.md`

### 28. All Abstractions Are Leaky
Software engineers retain advantage because they can fix leaks in AI-generated code. Knowledge of the layer below always helps. "Even the people who are in hardware engineering have an advantage if they understand the physics of what's going on." Source: `episodes/2026-02-ai/POLISHED.md`

### 29. Every Human Is a Spellcaster
AI as the great equalizer: "If you think of programmers as wizards who have memorized arcane commands, you can think of AI as a magic wand that's been handed to every person." Entrepreneurs have extreme agency; AI amplifies it. Source: `episodes/2026-02-ai/POLISHED.md`

### 30. The Entrepreneur's AI Immunity
"No entrepreneur is worried about AI taking their job" because being an entrepreneur isn't a job. AI is an ally for anyone doing something so difficult that any help is welcome. "The key thing that distinguishes entrepreneurs from everybody else is entrepreneurs have extreme agency." Source: `episodes/2026-02-ai/POLISHED.md`

### 31. Give Society What It Doesn't Know How to Get—at Scale
The definition of entrepreneurship: "Predicting that society will want it, and then figuring out how to scale it, and get it to everybody in a profitable way, in a self-sustaining way." Technology is just things that don't work yet. Source: `2019-03-14-product-scale.md`
