## When to Use

Use this when someone is anxious about AI taking their job, or when someone is trying to figure out which careers are "AI-proof." They may be a knowledge worker watching AI do increasingly sophisticated versions of their daily tasks. They may be a manager wondering if their team will be automated away. They feel the ground shifting and want to know where solid ground is.

This framework identifies the category of work that is structurally immune to AI displacement and explains why.

## The Framework

Naval makes a provocative claim:

> "No entrepreneur is worried about AI taking their job."

This is not because entrepreneurs are oblivious. It is because being an entrepreneur is not a job in the conventional sense:

> "Being an entrepreneur isn't a job. It's literally the opposite of a job."

A job is a defined slot in someone else's system. You fill the slot, perform the function, and collect compensation. AI can learn to fill slots. Entrepreneurs create systems. They define slots. They do the thing that does not yet have a process because the thing itself is new.

The core reason entrepreneurs are immune:

> "Entrepreneurs are trying to do impossible things. They're trying to do very difficult things. Any AI that shows up is their ally and can help them tackle this really hard problem."

When your work is so difficult that you welcome any help you can get, AI is not a threat — it is a force multiplier. The entrepreneur does not fear the AI assistant; they celebrate it. More leverage for the impossible thing they are building.

What AI lacks is agency — its own desires, survival instinct, and creative direction:

> "The thing that the AI itself is missing, at the end of the day, is its own creative agency. It's missing its own desires, and they have to be authentic, genuine desires."

This extends beyond formal entrepreneurs to anyone doing self-directed, difficult, creative work:

> "Whether you're a scientist or whether you're a true artist, or whether you are an entrepreneur, what you're trying to do is so difficult and is so self-directed that anything like an AI that can help you is a welcome ally."

Naval generalizes the point: the goal is not to have a job at all. Jobs are historical accidents — defined roles that existed because humans could not automate them yet. The trajectory is toward everyone doing self-directed work, with AI handling the mechanical parts.

## Example

Two people work in marketing. One is a marketing coordinator at a large company, executing templates: writing standard email campaigns, scheduling social posts, generating reports. AI can do all of this. She is at risk. The other is building a marketing agency from scratch, figuring out how to acquire clients in a new niche, experimenting with positioning, creating novel campaigns for problems no one has solved before. Every AI tool that arrives makes her more productive. She is not competing with AI. She is using it. The difference is not the domain. It is the relationship to the work: slot-filler versus system-creator.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Assess whether your current work is "filling a slot" (vulnerable) or "creating a system" (immune)
2. Shift your relationship to AI from competitor to ally by taking on harder, more self-directed problems
3. Recognize that the long-term goal is not to protect your job but to move beyond jobs entirely
4. Identify the specific ways AI can make your hardest current problem more tractable
