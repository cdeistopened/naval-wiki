## When to Use

Use this when someone is pursuing happiness as a destination, an achievement, or a peak emotional state. They are chasing the next vacation, the next purchase, the next milestone, believing that happiness is a feeling they will arrive at and stay in. They may also be confusing happiness with excitement, pleasure, or stimulation.

This framework redefines happiness as the absence of mental noise, not the presence of pleasure.

## The Framework

Naval's central redefinition:

> "When I say you want to be happy, what I'm actually saying is you want to find peace. We say peace of mind, but what we really want is peace from mind."

This is not wordplay. It is a diagnostic. Consider your moments of greatest pleasure:

> "During your moments of greatest pleasure—whether you're doing a drug, having an orgasm, finding your edge kite-surfing, laughing with a friend or looking at an incredible sunset—your mind goes quiet. It calms down, and that voice in your head goes silent. You achieve a sense of awe, which you might also call beauty, bliss or joy."

The common denominator in all peak experiences is not the external stimulus. It is the internal silence. The mind temporarily stops narrating, judging, wanting, and fearing. What remains is peace.

The problem is that the mind evolved to be a threat-detection machine:

> "Our mind evolved to be paranoid, fearful and angry. Humans are the most paranoid and angry creatures to ever walk the earth."

This was adaptive when a rustle in the bushes could be a tiger. It is maladaptive when you are lying in bed at 2 AM thinking about a Slack message. The threat level is not as high as your genes think.

Naval offers a formula that ties it together:

> "Peace is happiness at rest; happiness is peace in motion."

A person who is peaceful when doing nothing will naturally be happy when doing something. The goal is not to chase happiness through activity but to cultivate the underlying peace that makes activity enjoyable.

## Example

A product manager works 60-hour weeks, takes two expensive vacations a year, and exercises compulsively. She says she is happy during these activities but feels a persistent low-grade anxiety the rest of the time. The framework reveals that she is buying temporary silence for her mind through stimulation. The real work is developing the ability to sit quietly without the mind generating threat signals. That is the foundation. The activities can continue, but they stop being the source of happiness and become expressions of it.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Distinguish between pleasure (temporary mind-silencing through stimulation) and peace (the default state when nothing is wrong)
2. Notice that your happiest moments share one feature: the voice in your head went quiet
3. Stop treating happiness as something to pursue and start treating it as something to uncover
4. Recognize that the goal is peace, and happiness is what peace looks like in motion
