## When to Use

Use this when someone is arguing about whether AI is intelligent, or when someone is measuring their own intelligence by proxy metrics — test scores, degrees, job titles, verbal fluency. They may be overestimating AI because it sounds articulate, or underestimating themselves because they lack credentials. They may also be confused about what intelligence even means.

This framework provides a definition of intelligence that is both pragmatic and philosophically rigorous, and it exposes why AI fails the test.

## The Framework

Naval offers a definition that cuts through the academic debate:

> "The only true test of intelligence is if you get what you want out of life."

This is not anti-intellectual. It is anti-proxy. The G factor, IQ tests, SAT scores — these measure something real, but their value is predictive, not definitive. They correlate with life outcomes, but the outcomes are the actual test.

Intelligence, in this framing, is the compound effect of every decision you make:

> "It doesn't matter what your starting point is, because the world is so large now, and you can navigate it in so many different ways that every little choice you make compounds and demonstrates your ability to understand how the world works until you finally get to the place that you want."

This definition includes social intelligence, emotional intelligence, self-knowledge, taste, timing, and judgment. A person with a modest IQ who builds a life they genuinely love has passed the test. A person with a genius IQ who is miserable has not.

The interesting consequence is that AI fails this test by definition:

> "An AI doesn't have its own desires. It doesn't know what it wants out of life. It's not alive. It's not running for its own purposes."

Intelligence requires agency. You must have your own desires — authentic, genuine desires — to have something to be intelligent about. An AI can solve any problem you give it, but it cannot choose which problems matter. It has no survival instinct, no replication drive, no internal compass pointing toward "what I want."

This makes AI a tool of intelligence, not intelligence itself. The human provides the desire and the judgment about what matters. The AI provides execution leverage.

## Example

Two friends graduated from the same computer science program. One joined a top tech company, rose through the ranks, earns $800K, and is deeply unhappy — working on problems he does not care about, in a city he does not like, with no time for the things that matter to him. The other started a small software consultancy in a mountain town, earns $200K, and spends half his time skiing and building things he finds interesting. By conventional metrics, the first friend is more successful. By the only true test, the second friend is more intelligent — he got what he wanted out of life.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Define your own version of "what I want out of life" before measuring your intelligence by any proxy
2. Evaluate your decisions by whether they compound toward that definition
3. Stop being intimidated by AI's apparent intelligence — it has no desires and therefore cannot be intelligent in this sense
4. Recognize that the most important form of intelligence is self-knowledge: knowing what you actually want
