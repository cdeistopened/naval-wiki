## When to Use

Use this when someone knows what to do but is not doing it, or when someone has been doing the right things for six months and is frustrated that nothing has happened yet.
These are opposite problems with the same root cause: a miscalibrated relationship with time.
This framework gives the correct calibration.

## The Framework

Naval compresses this into a single paradox:

> "Impatience with actions, patience with results." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

### The Action Side: Zero Latency

When you see a problem, fix it now.
When you have an insight, act on it now.
When something is broken, do not schedule a meeting to discuss it next week.

> "If I discover a problem in one of my businesses, I won't sleep until the resolution is at least in motion." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-05-06-work-hard.md

This is not about working 80-hour weeks.
It is about zero latency between decision and action.
The decision to fix a problem and the first action toward fixing it should happen in the same hour, not the same quarter.

### The Results Side: Long Horizons

Complex systems -- markets, audiences, reputations, compound interest curves -- take years to produce visible outcomes.
Demanding results on a short timeline leads to abandoning good strategies prematurely.

Naval links this to the compound interest framework.
All returns in life come from compound interest.
Compound interest is invisible for years before it inflects.
If you are impatient with results, you will quit before the curve bends.

### The Self-Assessment

The practical implication is that you should judge yourself on actions, not outcomes.
Did you ship the product? Did you make the call? Did you write the post?
Those are within your control and should happen immediately.
Whether the product succeeds, the call converts, or the post goes viral is not within your control and may take months or years.

Daily question: "Am I acting immediately on everything within my control? And am I patient with everything outside my control?"
If both answers are yes, you are calibrated correctly.

### The Four Failure Modes

- **Patient with actions, impatient with results** = procrastinator who then panics about missed deadlines
- **Impatient with actions AND results** = burnout from frantic execution coupled with constant anxiety
- **Patient with actions AND results** = passive observer who does nothing and expects nothing
- **Impatient with actions, patient with results** = the correct pattern

### The Lion Connection

Naval ties this to his broader philosophy of working like a lion.
The lion sprints with maximum intensity when the opportunity appears, then rests.
The sprint is the impatience with actions -- zero latency between seeing the prey and launching.
The rest is the patience with results -- the lion does not panic if the next meal takes time to arrive.

### The Compounding Connection

Compounding requires two things: consistent action (impatience) and long time horizons (patience).
If you are missing either one, compounding does not work.
Without action, there is nothing to compound.
Without patience, you interrupt the compounding before it produces visible results.

## Example

A founder identifies a bug in her onboarding flow on Monday morning.
She does not schedule a sprint to address it next week.
She fixes it by Monday afternoon (impatient with actions).
She then launches a new content marketing strategy and does not check analytics for 90 days (patient with results).
Her competitor finds the same bug but puts it in the backlog.
He launches the same content strategy but checks analytics daily and pivots three times in the first month.
The founder compounds. The competitor oscillates.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Audit their relationship with time: are they too slow to act or too anxious about results?
- Identify one action they have been procrastinating on and do it today
- Identify one result they have been obsessing over and give it a longer timeline
- Build a daily habit of asking: "What can I act on right now?"
