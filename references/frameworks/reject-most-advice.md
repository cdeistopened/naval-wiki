## When to Use

Use this when you have consumed so much advice that you are paralyzed. You have read the books, listened to the podcasts, taken the courses, talked to the mentors. Every source contradicts the last. You are collecting wisdom without acting on any of it. Or you are following someone else's playbook so precisely that you have stopped thinking for yourself.

This framework gives you permission to discard most of what you hear and builds a filter for keeping the rest.

## The Framework

Naval's core observation is that successful people mostly give you their winning lottery numbers:

> "If you ask a successful person what worked for them, they often read out the exact set of things that worked for them, which might not apply to you. They're just reading you their winning lottery ticket numbers."

The solution is not to stop listening. It is to listen broadly and then decide independently:

> "The best founders I know read and listen to everyone. But then they ignore everyone and make up their own mind."

Naval describes a four-part filter for any piece of advice:

1. **Is it true?** Does this hold up to scrutiny on its own terms?
2. **Is it true outside their context?** Would this work if the specific circumstances were different?
3. **Is it true in my context?** Does this apply to my situation, skills, and constraints?
4. **Do I want to apply it?** Even if true, is this the right move for me right now?

If you survey enough people, all advice cancels to zero. That is the mathematical proof that you must develop your own point of view.

Naval reframes the purpose of advice entirely. It is not instruction to follow. It is a library of patterns to recognize later:

> "Ninety percent of my tweets are maxims that become mental hooks to remind me when I'm in the moment."

Advice becomes useful only when your own experience activates it. You read something, forget it, live through something, and then recall: "Ah, that's what that person meant." The advice was a seed. Your experience was the water.

> "The most dangerous part of taking advice is that the person who gave it to you isn't going to be around to tell you when it doesn't apply any more."

## Example

A first-time founder reads every startup playbook. Y Combinator says do things that do not scale. Peter Thiel says find a monopoly. Paul Graham says make something people want. Reid Hoffman says launch before you are ready. She has internalized all of it and is frozen. The reject-most-advice framework says: absorb all of it, then sit with your own situation. What is the specific constraint you face? Which of these maxims activates for your context? Build your own internal model and move.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Consume advice broadly without feeling obligated to follow any of it
2. Run any piece of advice through the four-part filter (true? true outside context? true in my context? worth applying?)
3. Treat maxims as mental hooks rather than instructions
4. Trust that your own experience will activate the right advice at the right time
