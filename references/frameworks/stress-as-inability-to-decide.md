## When to Use

Use this when someone describes themselves as stressed but cannot point to a single cause. They have too many things going on. They feel pulled in multiple directions. They want to relax but feel they should be working. They want to be present with family but keep thinking about the business. The stress is diffuse, chronic, and unresolvable through any single action.

This framework provides a precise physical definition of stress and a clear diagnostic.

## The Framework

Naval borrows from physics to define stress in mental terms:

> "In physical terms, stress happens when something wants to be in two places at one time. If I apply pressure to both ends of an iron beam, I create stress on the beam because one part wants to be north and the other part wants to be south."

Then the translation:

> "In mental terms, stress is an inability to decide what's important. You want two incompatible things at once. I want to relax, but I need to work. Now I'm under stress."

This is the diagnostic. Stress is not caused by having too much to do. It is caused by wanting two contradictory things simultaneously. The mind holds both desires in tension, and that tension is the stress.

The corollary is liberating:

> "When you give up on something, it's no longer stressful. When you accept that something's out of your control, there's no point in getting stressed about it."

Stress dissolves the moment you make a real decision. Not a compromise. Not a "I'll do both." A genuine choice where you accept the cost of what you are giving up.

Naval connects this to the broader project of peace:

> "You want to find peace from mind. You aren't turning off your brain. You can't suppress the mind or force it to do anything. If I say, 'Don't think of a white elephant,' you'll think of a white elephant. Rather, you want to develop tools so your mind can calm down on its own."

The mind creates stress by manufacturing false urgency and false trade-offs. Many of the things you believe are incompatible are only incompatible in the short term, or only incompatible in your imagination. The work is to examine whether the conflict is real.

## Example

A founder is stressed about fundraising. She wants to focus on product but knows she needs to raise money. She wants to spend time with her kids but feels guilty leaving the office. She wants to hire slowly but the board wants growth. Each of these is a pair of incompatible desires held simultaneously. The framework says: pick one in each pair. Decide that this month is fundraising month and product will wait. Decide that evenings are family time and the office will survive. The stress dissolves not because the situation changed, but because the internal conflict resolved.

## Output

After applying this framework, you should be able to:

1. Name the specific pair of incompatible desires creating your stress
2. Make a real decision between them, accepting the cost of what you are releasing
3. Distinguish between conflicts that are genuinely irreconcilable and conflicts that exist only in your mind
4. Recognize that stress is a signal to decide, not a signal to work harder
