## When to Use

Use this when someone has read Naval's ideas piecemeal and needs the ordered sequence. Specific knowledge, accountability, leverage, and judgment are not a buffet you pick from. They are a staircase. You climb them in order. Someone who tries to deploy leverage without specific knowledge will fail. Someone with specific knowledge but no accountability will remain invisible. This framework gives the progression and explains why order matters.

## The Framework

Naval's "How to Get Rich" tweetstorm encodes an ordered four-step sequence for wealth creation. Each step unlocks the next.

### Step 1: Specific Knowledge

The foundation. Specific knowledge is what you know that cannot be trained for in a classroom. It emerges from pursuing your genuine curiosity and innate talents, not from following the market.

> "Specific knowledge is found much more by pursuing your innate talents, your genuine curiosity, and your passion. It's not by going to school for whatever is the hottest job." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-25-specific-knowledge.md

The litmus test is visceral:

> "Building specific knowledge will feel like play to you but look like work to others." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-03-25-specific-knowledge.md

You cannot engineer this from first principles. Over-optimization toward money will push you into the wrong field. Naval warns against being too deliberate: "If you go around trying to build it a little too deliberately, if you become too goal-oriented on the money, then you won't pick the right thing."

Specific knowledge can be timely (expertise in machine learning when ML is hot) or timeless (persuasion, negotiation, judgment). The timeless forms are more durable but harder to monetize immediately.

### Step 2: Accountability

Once you have specific knowledge, you must attach your name to it. Take credit publicly. Accept the risk of failure publicly. This is the step most people skip because it is frightening.

> "Embrace accountability and take business risks under your own name. Society will reward you with responsibility, equity, and leverage." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-11-accountability-leverage.md

Accountability is reputational skin in the game. When you put your name on a project, you gain the upside of credit and bear the downside of blame. This double-edged nature is exactly what makes it valuable: it separates you from everyone hiding behind corporate anonymity.

> "Taking accountability for your actions is the same as taking an equity position in all of your work." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-13-accountability-equity.md

The market rewards people who can fail publicly. Integrity failures (fraud, dishonesty) are permanent. Economic failures (a startup that did not work) are forgiven and even respected.

### Step 3: Leverage

Leverage is the force multiplier applied to your specific knowledge. It comes in three forms: labor (people working for you), capital (money working for you), and products with zero marginal cost of replication (code and media).

> "Fortunes require leverage. Business leverage comes from capital, people and products with no marginal costs of replication." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-15-labor-capital.md

Code and media are permissionless -- you do not need anyone's approval to deploy them. Labor and capital require someone else's buy-in. The permissionless forms are where most new wealth is being created.

### Step 4: Judgment

With leverage applied, the quality of your decisions determines everything. Judgment is knowing the long-term consequences of your actions. It is the most valuable skill in an age of infinite leverage because small differences in decision quality get amplified enormously.

> "In an age of infinite leverage, judgment becomes the most important skill." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-29-judgment.md

> "Leverage is a force multiplier for your judgment." — Naval Ravikant, 2019-04-29-judgment.md

Judgment is the final multiplier. A person with specific knowledge, public accountability, and code leverage who makes poor decisions will still fail. A person who makes excellent decisions will compound their advantages.

The sequence matters because each step depends on the one before it. You cannot deploy leverage wisely without specific knowledge. You will not attract leverage without accountability. And judgment without leverage is just wisdom with no outlet.

## Example

A product designer (specific knowledge in user experience for financial tools) starts writing publicly about fintech design patterns (accountability under her own name). She builds a free open-source design system that thousands of fintech startups adopt (code leverage with zero marginal cost). When a Series B fintech needs a head of design, she is the obvious choice, and she negotiates equity rather than salary (judgment applied to a high-leverage decision). She has climbed all four steps in order.

Had she tried to negotiate equity at step 1 without the public reputation (step 2) or the design system (step 3), nobody would have taken her seriously.

## Output

After reading this, a person should be able to:
- Identify which step of the sequence they are currently on
- Name their specific knowledge (or identify that they need to find it)
- Evaluate whether they have enough public accountability attached to their work
- Choose which form of leverage to pursue next
- Understand why skipping steps leads to failure
